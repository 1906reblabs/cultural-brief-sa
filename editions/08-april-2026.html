The City as Canvas: Public Space, Light, and the Question of Who the Street Belongs To

LUMENOCITY opens tomorrow in the Company's Garden — Africa's first light art festival, built entirely by South African artists, transforming a colonial garden into something the colonists could not have imagined. In Venice in a month, a pavilion will remain empty while Koyo Kouoh's exhibition opens around it. Freedom Month deepens. This week asks what public space is for, and who gets to fill it.

"The struggle is also a creation."
— Aimé Césaire, Notebook of a Return to the Native Land, 1939

Thursday in South Africa. Tomorrow night, the Company's Garden in Cape Town — a public space whose history carries the full weight of what colonialism did to the land it seized — will be lit by twenty-plus large-scale artworks made entirely by South African artists. LUMENOCITY opens its debut edition from 9 to 12 April, transforming the museum precinct at the top of Adderley Street into something the space was not designed for: an open-air gallery of light, accessible to anyone who walks through its gates.

The timing is not accidental, even if it is only partly intentional. LUMENOCITY was scheduled alongside the Two Oceans Marathon weekend — a pragmatic alignment with the largest gathering of visitors in the city's autumn calendar. But it arrives in a week that carries other meanings. Freedom Month continues. The Venice Biennale preview is thirty-one days away, and South Africa has confirmed, definitively, that its pavilion will remain empty. In the Company's Garden, twenty metres from the South African Museum and the National Gallery, local artists will projection-map the facades of colonial institutions with images conceived in 2026. The resonance is unplanned and real.

The question this edition's long essay holds is not whether LUMENOCITY is art in the serious sense — that question is premature, and the answer will depend on what the installations actually do. The question is what the act of illuminating public space means in a country where public space has historically been allocated by race, controlled by permit, and managed in the interests of the few. When Cape Town's Company's Garden is transformed, nightly, for four nights, into a place where people come after dark without the instruction of an institution — without a curator telling them what it means, without a ticket to a white cube — something structurally different is happening. Whether the work is good enough to match that structural difference is the question to ask of each installation individually.

AfrikaBurn opens in nineteen days in the Tankwa Karoo. The Freedom Day weekend that follows will bring the cultural calendar into its most politically freighted stretch. Venice opens nine days after that. The weeks ahead accumulate significance faster than the country can process them. This edition attends to what is happening now: a garden lit by South African hands, a pavilion that will hold no South African voice, and the slow, ongoing argument about what public space is for.

Tomorrow night, light art made entirely by South African artists opens in the Company's Garden — a space the VOC laid out in 1652 for the provisioning of passing ships. In 2026, the ships do not stop here. The people do.

Curated Dispatches

LUMENOCITY — Africa's First Light Art Festival, Company's Garden

Cape Town  ·  9–12 April  ·  From 17:30 nightly

LUMENOCITY opens tomorrow evening in the Company's Garden, running through Sunday. The festival is billed as Africa's first dedicated celebration of light-based art and immersive creative technology — a claim that may or may not survive scrutiny once the continent's full programme history is examined, but which reflects a genuine ambition. The 2026 edition was designed and produced entirely by South African artists and practitioners, a fact the organisers foreground with the kind of emphasis that suggests they understand what distinguishes this from the international light festival circuit.

The centrepiece is a ten-metre light structure housing a 5×5 metre LED cave — a full visual immersion environment that responds to movement and sound. Alongside it: projection mapping by Cape Town illustrator and multidisciplinary artist Yay Abe across the façades of the South African Museum and the National Gallery. More than twenty large-scale installations and interactive artworks are distributed through the garden, with a "Beam of Hope" light column rising into the Cape Town skyline for the duration.

The choice of the Company's Garden as venue is worth holding carefully. The garden was established in 1652 by the Dutch East India Company for the provisioning of ships passing the Cape — its founding logic was colonial extraction. The institution that now occupies its upper end, the National Gallery, holds a collection assembled in part through the cultural logic of the same colonial period. Projection-mapping the gallery's exterior with artwork conceived by South African artists in 2026 is not, by itself, a decolonial act. But it is a reorientation of the building's relationship to the night, to the street, and to the city that has always surrounded it. Whether the works themselves carry the conceptual weight that this setting implies — whether Yay Abe's projections say something about the building rather than simply beautifying it — is the question that the opening will begin to answer.

What is structurally interesting about LUMENOCITY — and what distinguishes it from gallery programming — is its relationship to access. The garden is a public space. The festival's founding rhetoric foregrounds accessibility and community engagement alongside the artistic programme. Tickets from R150 are not nothing, but they are not the cultural pricing of a gala evening. Four nights, nightly from dusk, family-friendly, food trucks, people moving through illuminated trees: the format is deliberately permeable in a way that the white cube and the ticketed auditorium are not.

This is its first edition. The first edition of anything serious is interesting primarily for what it promises and for how well the production holds together under the weight of its own ambition. The organisers have a five-year vision that includes expanding across the full Company's Garden precinct, developing citywide light trails, and — in the most ambitious projection — mapping Table Mountain. Whether this festival becomes a durable institution, or whether it is one of the genuinely interesting debuts that do not recur, depends on what the work is, and whether the city continues to hold the space for it.

LUMENOCITY, Company's Garden, Cape Town. 9–12 April, gates 17:30–23:30 nightly. Tickets from R150 at Quicket. lumenocity.co.

The Venice Confirmation — What the Empty Pavilion Now Means

Arts Policy  ·  Venice Biennale, Preview 6–8 May

The confirmation is now complete. South Africa will not hold a pavilion at the 61st Venice Biennale. The Art Periodic and DSAC partnership — the sector-led model announced in March as a replacement for the original Goliath selection — has also collapsed, with Latitudes Online issuing a public withdrawal statement confirming that the proposed pavilion will not proceed and that the partnership between A°P and DSAC was terminated by the department. The pavilion space in the Arsenale will be empty when the Biennale opens on 9 May.

It is worth marking this clearly, because the weeks since the March edition have resolved what was then still uncertain. The second process — the open call, the sector-led advisory committee, the announced governance reform — has not produced a pavilion. The department that terminated the first selection process has also ended the second. South Africa's absence at Venice is not a single ministerial decision: it is the cumulative result of a department that could not sustain two successive attempts to organise its own participation.

What this costs, culturally, is not easily quantified. Venice is not the only site of international cultural exchange, and the quality of what South Africa produces domestically is not diminished by the empty pavilion. But the Arsenale space will be occupied by sixty-eight other national pavilions. The world's most influential contemporary art event will open on 9 May, curated by a woman who did her most significant work in Cape Town, who built the institution that housed the largest collection of African contemporary art in the world, and whose posthumous exhibition will be the first thing serious art visitors encounter in the Giardini. In that context, South Africa's absence is a specific kind of statement — not the one the department intended, but the one its actions have produced.

Gabrielle Goliath has said she will find another space in Venice to show Elegy. The work is not contingent on the pavilion. It existed before the pavilion; it will exist after the pavilion is empty. What is contingent on the pavilion is South Africa's capacity to say, publicly and in the world's most visible art forum, what kind of country it is and what kind of culture it supports. That capacity has been exhausted for this cycle.

Venice Biennale Arte 2026. Preview days 6–8 May. Public opening 9 May. latitudes.online for background on the pavilion withdrawal. artthrob.co.za for the selection committee's original statement.

AfrikaBurn — Tankwa Karoo, 27 April–3 May

Northern Cape  ·  Advance Notice  ·  Tickets Required

AfrikaBurn opens in nineteen days in the Tankwa Karoo — the nineteen-year-old desert gathering that is, at its best, South Africa's most rigorous annual experiment in non-transactional creative community. The 2026 poster artist is Fazel Ockhuys. The installation programme, as noted in the previous edition, includes work produced in dialogue with archival and community-based processes that sit with unusual coherence in a Freedom Month context.

A note on editorial restraint: this publication has previously and will continue to approach AfrikaBurn with caution. It is large, it generates hype, and the coverage it attracts is typically about the spectacle rather than the substance. What is worth attending to is the immersive art programme — specifically the large-scale installations, several of which this year engage questions of land, memory, and collective identity that are not separate from the political calendar in which they are made. The music infrastructure is secondary to that contribution. For readers who will be there: the morning hours, before the programme energy concentrates, are when the installations can be encountered on something closer to their own terms.

AfrikaBurn, Tankwa Karoo. 27 April – 3 May. afrikaburn.org. Tickets required; limited availability remaining.

Sally Andrew's Wild Things Never Die — April Publication

Literature  ·  Out This Month

Sally Andrew's new Tannie Maria novel, Wild Things Never Die, publishes this month ahead of the Franschhoek Literary Festival in May, where Andrew will be among the speakers. The Tannie Maria series — set in the Klein Karoo, organised around recipes and murder and the complex social texture of a small Afrikaans town — has sold internationally and built an audience that extends well beyond the South African literary market.

This edition notes it not to pre-review the novel, but for a structural observation: Andrew's work demonstrates that South African popular fiction, written from a distinctly South African location and refusing to translate its specificity for overseas audiences, can find international readership without institutional support and without the validation of the major literary prizes. The Karoo setting, the Afrikaans community, the character's particular relationship to food and consolation and local knowledge — none of these require explanation; they carry. That capacity is undertheorised in South African literary discourse, which tends to attend to the serious novel and the prize-winning work while the infrastructure of popular fiction quietly builds its own relationship with readers. Wild Things Never Die is the fifth Tannie Maria novel. Five is an institution of a certain kind.

Wild Things Never Die by Sally Andrew. Published April 2026 by Penguin Random House. Available at independent booksellers and major retailers.

Long Essay

The Garden and the Gallery: On Public Space, Light, and What South African Cities Hold

The Company's Garden was planted in 1652 by Jan van Riebeeck, who arrived at the Cape with three ships and the mandate of the Dutch East India Company to establish a refreshment station for passing fleets. The garden he laid out — vegetables, fruit trees, a system of waterways — was not intended as a gift to anyone. It was a logistical installation: a mechanism for the extraction of food from a landscape, using enslaved labour, in service of a commercial empire. That the space became, over time, a public park — that it is now walked by families and students and office workers during their lunch breaks, that it contains oak trees and squirrels and a small café — is a fact of a particular kind. It is the transformation of a colonial installation into a civic amenity, carried out so gradually that the original function has become invisible to most of the people who use the space.

The South African Museum sits at one end of the garden. It was established in 1825, making it the oldest museum in sub-Saharan Africa. Its collections include natural history specimens, San rock art casts, and, until the early 2000s, a display of human remains — the bodies of Khoikhoi and San people, held in the museum's ethnographic collection — that became one of the founding cases in the global debate about the repatriation of human remains from colonial institutions. The museum has, in recent years, worked to address that history, to transform its interpretive frameworks, to understand itself as a site of reckoning rather than a repository of past certainties. Whether that transformation is complete, or whether it is ongoing in ways that have structural bite rather than cosmetic effect, is a question the institution is still living with.

The National Gallery is adjacent. Its permanent collection contains Cape Dutch landscapes, Victorian portraits, and, in its contemporary wing, work by South African artists that has expanded steadily in quality and range over the past three decades. It is a public institution. It is free on Sundays. It is less visited than it deserves to be, which is a fact about the city rather than about the gallery.

Tomorrow night, Yay Abe will project images onto the facades of both institutions. The precise nature of the projections has not been detailed in the festival's promotional materials — the language is of "animation" and "living art" and the transformation of "historic architecture." What this means in practice will become clear when the installations begin. But the structural act is already legible: a South African artist, working in 2026, will use light to temporarily reimpose their own vision on buildings whose permanent external appearance carries the visual grammar of empire.

This is not a new gesture in the history of public art. Projection mapping on institutional facades has been done across the world, in contexts ranging from state commemoration to protest. Its politics vary with the content of the projections and the relationships between the artist, the institution, and the commissioning body. LUMENOCITY is a commercial festival with a five-year business plan and a partnership with Mini South Africa. The projections are not protest; they are programming. What distinguishes programming from protest is not always the content — it is the question of who has authorised the act and what the authorisation costs.

And yet the question of public space is not reducible to the question of authorisation. The history of South African cities is, in significant part, a history of the control of public space by the state and by capital — of who was permitted to walk where, to gather in which squares, to use which parks. The pass laws that Sharpeville's marchers were protesting in 1960 were instruments of this control: mechanisms for determining which bodies were permitted to move through which spaces, and under what conditions. The informal settlements at the edges of South African cities — the product of the spatial engineering of apartheid, and of the failure to reverse that engineering after 1994 — are another form of this history. Where people live, and how far they must travel to reach the spaces where cultural life happens, is a question of public space that no light festival resolves.

But it can, at minimum, ask. A festival that turns the Company's Garden into an open-air gallery for four nights — that replaces the daytime logic of families eating sandwiches on benches with the evening logic of adults and children moving through illuminated trees in a space that does not require them to know art history — is participating in a conversation about what public space is for. The conversation is not completed by the festival. It is extended by it.

South Africa's cultural infrastructure is, in the main, concentrated in institutions. The museum, the gallery, the theatre, the concert hall: these are the containers the country inherited from colonial and apartheid-era cultural policy, and they are the containers into which most of the public cultural funding flows. That concentration has its logic — institutions maintain collections, train practitioners, sustain programming across cycles — but it also has its costs. Institutions are buildings in particular places, with particular hours and particular ticket prices, and their location within the spatial logic of South African cities means that access to them is distributed unequally. This is not a new observation. It is the observation that drives every argument for outdoor concerts, community theatre programmes, mobile libraries, and arts education funding in schools. The argument is correct, and it is chronically underfunded.

What light festivals offer, at their best, is a temporary inversion of this logic. Not a solution — a temporary inversion. The garden is there. The museum facade is there. The National Gallery is there. The addition of light and projection and interactive artworks transforms the relationship between those existing elements and the people who encounter them: it says that this space, for these four nights, is organised around the encounter between the public and the work, not the work and the institution. Whether the organisers of LUMENOCITY understand this distinction clearly enough to have programmed for it — whether the works they have commissioned carry the conceptual weight that the setting implies, or whether they are primarily visually impressive installations that do not engage the history of the space they inhabit — is the critical question.

It is a question that cannot be answered by the festival's promotional materials. It can only be answered by the installations themselves, encountered in the dark, by people who are willing to ask what the light is showing them and not merely admire that the light exists.

The Company's Garden has been illuminated before. Every year, the Cape Town CBD is lit at Christmas in the service of the retail calendar. The logistical logic of the VOC has been replaced, over three and a half centuries, by the logistical logic of consumption. LUMENOCITY is neither of these things — it is not liturgical, it is not commercial in the way that retail light installations are commercial. It is an art programme in a public space, made by South African artists, in a month that is simultaneously Freedom Month and the month before the Venice Biennale opens without the country that nurtured its lead curator. That context does not determine the quality of the work. But it is the context in which the work will be encountered, whether the festival acknowledges it or not.

The artists who plant seeds in colonial soil do not always know what will grow. Yay Abe, projecting images onto the South African Museum tomorrow night, is working in a long tradition of South African creative practice that has refused to treat inherited institutions as settled — that has insisted on asking, through the making, what these buildings are for now, and who they serve. That insistence is not guaranteed to produce great art. But it is the right question, held in the right place, on the right night.

In the morning, the garden will look as it always does. The VOC's grid will still be there, the oak trees, the café, the museum's Victorian roofline. The projections will be off. The LED cave will be packed down. The Beam of Hope will not be shining into the city sky. The question is whether the encounter with the light — the experience of moving through a space that was briefly reorganised around the imagination of local artists rather than the logic of the institution that holds it — leaves anything behind. That residue is what public art at its most serious produces, and it is impossible to produce in advance. It can only be discovered by going, in the dark, and seeing what is there.

Closing Reflection

The Company's Garden was planted to feed the ships of an empire. The empire is gone. The garden is still there, used by the city in the ways that cities use the spaces they have inherited without having chosen them.

Tomorrow night it will be lit by the imagination of South African artists working in 2026, in a month called Freedom Month, thirty-one days before a global art event opens in Venice under a curatorial vision conceived by an African woman who is not here to see it. The light will be extraordinary or it will be merely beautiful. Either way, the act of filling that space with local hands and local light is not nothing. It is, in fact, quite specifically something. Go and see what it is.
