The Cultural Brief (SA)

"The truth isn't always beauty, but the hunger for it is." — Nadine Gordimer

Something significant has been settled this week — not with a verdict, exactly, but with an absence. On 20 February, the Department of Sport, Arts and Culture confirmed what many had feared since January: South Africa will not hold a pavilion at the 61st Venice Biennale. For the first time since 2011, the country will send no one. The room will be empty.

This is the cultural fact that shadows everything else this week. It is worth sitting with its full weight before moving on to anything lighter. South Africa — a democracy forged, in part, by the international solidarity of artists, writers and performers who refused to allow apartheid to go unnamed on the world's stages — has chosen, through its minister of sport, arts and culture, to absent itself from the world's most prestigious contemporary art platform. The reason: a single artist's insistence on mourning the dead of Gaza alongside the dead of South Africa and Namibia.

Minister Gayton McKenzie's December letter to Art Periodic described the Gaza component of Gabrielle Goliath's Elegy as "highly divisive in nature." In February, the North Gauteng High Court dismissed Goliath and curator Ingrid Masondo's urgent application to reinstate the pavilion, awarding costs to McKenzie's side. No reasons were given. The court did not explain what it found unpersuasive about the constitutional freedom of expression arguments, or what it found persuasive in the minister's claim — advanced largely through contractual rather than rights-based framing — that he possessed effective veto power over independently curated art made in the name of the nation.

What is left, for now, is a vacancy. A room in Venice where South African ideas should have been, and are not. This week's edition holds that vacancy in mind. It is not the only thing worth attention — the Folklore Festival Fringe begins tomorrow; Imraan Coovadia launches what may be his most politically pointed novel tonight at The Book Lounge; Spier Light Art opens in Stellenbosch. The cultural life of this country continues, as it always has, with or without official sanction. But the empty pavilion is the frame through which this week's dispatches are best read.

This Week's Cultural Signal

South Africa's pavilion at the Venice Biennale will remain empty. The court's silence — no reasoning given — amplifies McKenzie's silence about contradiction. Gabrielle Goliath has said she will find another space in Venice to show Elegy regardless. The work, she insists, "is not contingent on any particular platform." Neither, it turns out, is the suppression of it.

Curated Dispatches

Tonight in Cape Town — Imraan Coovadia Launches An Enemy of the People

Imraan Coovadia's new novel arrives tonight at The Book Lounge in Cape Town, where he'll be in conversation with writer and editor Will Shoki. The title is borrowed from Ibsen — a deliberate provocation from one of South Africa's most rigorous literary minds. Coovadia, who teaches creative writing at UCT, has spent the better part of two decades building a body of work that refuses the consolations of the post-apartheid redemption narrative. The Institute for Taxi Poetry, Tales of the Metric System, Authority — each has mapped a South Africa in which institutions corrode and individual conscience becomes an increasingly lonely position.

That An Enemy of the People arrives in the same week as a court ruling that effectively subordinates artistic conscience to ministerial preference is either coincidence or a kind of dark luck. In Ibsen's original, Dr Stockmann insists on telling an inconvenient truth and is destroyed for it by the community he sought to serve. The structure resonates.

The Book Lounge, Roeland Street, Cape Town. Thursday 05 March, 6pm. Free. No booking required.

Folklore Festival Fringe 2026 — #KINFOLK

Pilani Bubu's Folklore Festival Fringe begins its 2026 five-city roadshow tomorrow, running through to 9 April under the theme #KINFOLK. Rather than drawing artists to a single urban festival hub, the Fringe travels to them — to Durban, Johannesburg, Pretoria, Polokwane and Gqeberha — with structured industry feedback, curated networking, and platform for four emerging regional artists per city across an evening of music, poetry, film and dance.

The Fringe is supported by the National Arts Council and positions itself as an infrastructure project as much as a performance one. In a week dominated by news of what state power chose to suppress, the Fringe is a reminder of what emerges in the spaces the state does not fully control: patient, community-rooted cultural work that asks how artists survive, not just whether they are heard.

Folklore Festival Fringe 2026, five cities, 6 March – 9 April.

Spier Light Art — Eighth Edition, Opening Tomorrow

Now in its eighth year, Spier Light Art transforms the Stellenbosch wine farm into a nocturnal exhibition of contemporary work exploring how light shapes memory, place, power and possibility. The exhibition runs from tomorrow, 6 March, through to 6 April, with guided tours on Tuesday, Thursday and Sunday evenings at 7:30pm.

The outdoor setting — vineyards, dam, river, historic farm buildings — remains one of the more honest contexts in which to encounter art in the Western Cape. No white cube, no pretension of neutrality; the landscape is always part of the reading.

Spier Wine Farm, Baden Powell Drive, Stellenbosch. From 6:30pm nightly.

Books on the Bay — Mark the Calendar

Next week, 13–15 March, Simon's Town's historic venues host the Books on the Bay festival — one of the more serious literary weekends in the Western Cape's annual calendar. The 2026 line-up includes Antjie Krog, Fred Khumalo, Karen Jennings, Mandy Wiener and Don Pinnock, among others. Sessions are held across the Naval Museum Dockyard Chapel, St Francis Church, the Town Hall and the Methodist Church.

Krog's presence in particular warrants attention. Her recent work has continued its long engagement with land, language and the violence underwriting both.

Books on the Bay, Simon's Town, 13–15 March 2026.

Long Essay

The Empty Room: On the Goliath Affair and the State's Idea of South Africa

The South African pavilion at the 61st Venice Biennale will be empty. This fact, confirmed on 20 February by the Department of Sport, Arts and Culture, ends a sequence of events that began in December 2025 with a ministerial letter, accelerated in January with a cancellation, moved briefly into a courtroom in February, and resolved — if resolved is the right word — in vacancy.

An independent committee, operating under the auspices of Art Periodic, selected Johannesburg-based artist Gabrielle Goliath and curator Ingrid Masondo to represent South Africa at Venice. Their proposal was an extension of Elegy, a long-running, multi-site performance work that Goliath has been developing since 2015. The work is a polyphonic lament — built around female opera singers — that centres femicide and the killing of LGBTQI+ people in South Africa. The 2026 iteration would extend the work's mourning to include Namibian women killed during the German-perpetuated Herero and Nama genocide of the early twentieth century, and Palestinian women and children killed in the ongoing war in Gaza. Specifically, Elegy proposed to include a tribute to Hiba Abu Nada, a Palestinian poet killed by an Israeli airstrike in October 2023.

On 22 December, Minister Gayton McKenzie wrote to Art Periodic. The Gaza component, he said, was "highly divisive in nature and relates to an ongoing international conflict that is widely polarizing." He requested a change in curatorial direction. Goliath refused. On 2 January, McKenzie cancelled the pavilion and terminated the department's contract with Art Periodic. Goliath and Masondo took the matter to the North Gauteng High Court, arguing that McKenzie lacked contractual authority over the pavilion's artistic content, and that his intervention violated their constitutional right to freedom of expression. On 18 February, Judge Mamoloko Kubushi dismissed their application without providing reasons, and awarded costs to the respondents. Days later, the department confirmed South Africa would not participate in the Biennale at all.

It is worth pausing on what is unusual here, before moving to what is familiar. What is unusual is the internal contradiction that McKenzie has not resolved publicly. South Africa is the country that, in January 2024, stood before the International Court of Justice and argued that Israel's conduct in Gaza constituted genocide. That position reflects a foreign policy rooted in the liberation movement's own experience of international isolation and the solidarity of those who refused to accept that some lives are more grievable than others.

Goliath's Elegy is, in its structure and logic, an expression of precisely that moral framework. It asks: whose deaths are permitted to register as deaths? Whose losses may be named and mourned in art, and whose must be silenced for the comfort of others? That the minister of a government which brought a genocide case to the ICJ would suppress an artwork that mourns the same dead — without seeming to register the contradiction — is not merely inconsistent. It is revealing.

What it reveals is the difference between foreign policy and cultural policy; between the solidarity declared by governments in international forums and the solidarity tolerated when it appears in art, in a national pavilion, where it might be seen by the world. McKenzie's stated rationale — that the pavilion exists to "sell the country to the world," to project cohesion rather than engage polarising debates — is a perfectly coherent position if you believe that national cultural representation is primarily a branding exercise. It is an incoherent position if you believe, as South Africa's constitution does, that freedom of artistic expression is a right.

What is familiar, in this episode, is more uncomfortable to name. South Africa has a long history of the state defining what counts as an acceptable national story. Post-1994, the assumption — reinforced by a constitution that explicitly protects freedom of expression and artistic creativity — was that this logic had been dismantled. The Goliath affair makes visible that this assumption was always contingent — dependent on ministers who understood and honoured the distinction between the state's ownership of a pavilion and its authority over the art that fills it. McKenzie did not honour that distinction.

The precedent this sets is not hypothetical. Future ministers now know that a South African court has not prevented a culture minister from exercising a content veto over independently selected work destined for a national platform. The mechanism exists. It has been used. It has survived legal challenge.

There is something in Goliath's response that deserves to be considered carefully. She has said she will find another space in Venice to show Elegy, regardless of the pavilion's fate. Art does not require state endorsement to exist, to move people, to insist on the grievability of the dead. What it requires is — as Goliath puts it — conditions of care: a room, an audience, attention, seriousness. Those conditions can be assembled independently of ministerial approval.

The question that outlasts this specific episode is structural. It concerns how South Africa selects and governs its cultural representations in international contexts — and whether the principle of independent curatorial authority can be made structurally durable, rather than dependent on ministerial goodwill. The department has indicated it intends to develop a revised, sector-led model for future Venice Biennale participation. This is welcome, if it is genuine. But the model will be worth very little without provisions that are explicit about the limits of ministerial content intervention.

The room will be empty. The work will exist elsewhere. The question of what South Africa was willing to say about itself — and what it chose to suppress — will remain on the record.

Closing Reflection

Hiba Abu Nada was a Palestinian poet, novelist and teacher. She was thirty-two years old when she was killed in an Israeli airstrike in October 2023. She had won prizes. She had written a novel. She had, in the days before her death, posted a poem on social media that people translated and shared in many languages.

Gabrielle Goliath wanted to mourn her. In a work already built around mourning — around the South African women killed by the men who were supposed to love them, around the Namibian women killed by a colonial power — she wanted to include one more name. The state said no. That refusal is now the loudest thing in the room.
