After the Opening: On What the World Heard, and What the Zeitz Must Now Answer

The Venice Biennale has been open five days. "In Minor Keys" is visible to the world, and the first wave of critical response confirms what Koyo Kouoh understood the exhibition could do. The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa — the institution she built — continues without a named permanent successor. Africa Day is eleven days away. Cape Town Opera opens Carmen at Artscape on Tuesday. The question the sector cannot defer is no longer about mourning. It is about building.

"Art is man's constant effort to create for himself a different order of reality from that which is given to him."
— Chinua Achebe, The Novelist as Teacher, 1965

Thursday in South Africa, and the Venice Biennale is five days old. The public opening was Saturday, 9 May — one day short of a year after Koyo Kouoh died. In the Giardini and the Arsenale and the palazzi and the vaporetto queues and the private dinners of the art world's most concentrated week, the exhibition she curated is now being read, argued about, and absorbed by the people whose institutions and publications and collecting decisions will carry its influence forward. The first assessment has arrived: that "In Minor Keys" is not what anyone expected from a Venice Biennale central exhibition, and that the unexpectedness is the point.

The critical apparatus moves fast in the Venice preview period and settles slowly over the months that follow. What the first week of response shows is not a verdict but a beginning — a set of initial orientations that signal what the exhibition is doing to the assumptions the global art world brought to the Giardini. The reviews are arriving. The conversations are happening. South Africa is in them, in the form of an absence, in the form of the institution that produced the curator, in the form of the question that every serious piece of writing about "In Minor Keys" is now obliged to at least note: where is the South African pavilion?

In Cape Town, in the Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa, the staff who built the institution with Kouoh and who have carried it through seventeen months of transition are now in the specific position of watching the world engage with the fullest expression of what they collectively produced — and doing so without a named leader who can speak publicly to what comes next. That position is no longer sustainable on deferral alone. Venice has changed the terms. The world now has a clear, visible, publicly accessible account of what the institution produces at its maximum capacity. The board knows this. The appointment decision is now a statement about whether the institution understands what it has.

This edition carries four dispatches: the first week of "In Minor Keys" and what it reveals; Gabrielle Goliath's Elegy, showing this week in Venice outside the pavilion structure; Cape Town Opera's new production of Carmen at Artscape, opening Tuesday; and the National Arts Festival programme, now confirmed for Makhanda in late June. The long essay takes up the Zeitz succession as an institutional argument — the most consequential governance question in the South African cultural sector, and the one that Venice has made impossible to hold at arm's length any longer.

Africa Day is eleven days away. The Achebe line above is not epigraph only — it names what Kouoh was doing, what Goliath's Elegy is doing, what the festival in Makhanda and the opera in Cape Town are doing. The effort to construct a different order of reality from what is given. That is the work. It does not stop because the person who held the clearest vision of it is gone.

"In Minor Keys" is five days old, and the world is beginning to hear what Koyo Kouoh composed. The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa — the institution she built to make that composing possible — has no public account of what comes next. Five days is not long. The Biennale runs until November. By then, the silence will have become its own answer.

Curated Dispatches

"In Minor Keys" — What the First Week of Critical Reception Shows

Venice Biennale  ·  Open through 22 November 2026

The critical response to "In Minor Keys" is in its first week, and the pattern it is establishing is worth naming early: what the world's art press is encountering in the Giardini is an exhibition that refuses the logic by which Venice central exhibitions have typically organised themselves — the encyclopedic survey, the thematic argument illustrated by geographic diversity, the curatorial voice that positions itself as a neutral convener of global perspectives. Kouoh's exhibition does none of this. It proposes, instead, a form of curatorial intelligence that operates by affinity rather than representation, by emotional logic rather than institutional grammar, by what she described as the "natural habitat of the arts" — a space of nourishment and joy and communal repair — rather than the white-cube neutrality that the Biennale's infrastructure normally enforces.

The critical disagreement is already visible, and it is a productive disagreement: between those who read the exhibition's atmospheric, immersive, processional character as a radical formal innovation, and those who read it as a deliberate refusal of the Biennale's own power to confer legitimacy — a refusal made from inside the most legitimacy-conferring space in the art world. Both readings are correct, and the tension between them is exactly what Kouoh would have wanted: an exhibition that cannot be absorbed into the critical vocabulary that was waiting for it.

What is not in critical disagreement is the quality of the individual selections. The homage to Issa Samb and Beverly Buchanan at the heart of the Central Pavilion — two artists who worked their entire careers against monumental logic — is being consistently described as one of the most intelligent curatorial gestures in recent Biennale history: a statement about the exhibition's values made visible before the visitor has seen a single work curated for its own sake. The procession motif that organises the physical experience of moving through the Giardini has produced, by multiple accounts, something the space has rarely produced: the feeling of being accompanied rather than assessed.

South Africa's absence is present in the critical record as a named fact, not a footnote. Several of the most widely read initial reviews place it at the front of their accounts: the country whose institution nurtured the curator, whose cultural politics suppressed the artwork selected for its own pavilion, is absent from the most visible cultural event of the year. The critical record will carry this for the exhibition's six-month run. What the country does with that record — whether it treats it as a spur to structural reform or as an embarrassment to be managed past — is the question the sector will be answering through the rest of this year.

Venice Biennale Arte 2026. "In Minor Keys," Central Exhibition. Open daily through 22 November 2026. Giardini and Arsenale, Venice. labiennale.org.

Gabrielle Goliath — Elegy in Venice

Venice  ·  Running through May

Gabrielle Goliath's Elegy is in Venice. The work that was selected twice by independent committees to represent South Africa at the 61st Biennale, cancelled twice by ministerial intervention, and never installed in the Arsenale pavilion that was built for it, is being shown in an alternative space in the city during the Biennale's opening weeks. The confirmation that Goliath found another route to Venice — that the work exists in the city, in May, alongside the Biennale's most concentrated critical audience — is the clearest possible demonstration of what she said when the pavilion was cancelled: that the work is not contingent on any particular platform.

Elegy is a polyphonic lament built around female opera singers, centring femicide and the killing of LGBTQI+ people in South Africa. The 2026 iteration extends the mourning to include Namibian women killed during the German-perpetrated Herero and Nama genocide, and Palestinian women killed in the ongoing war in Gaza — specifically the tribute to Hiba Abu Nada, the Palestinian poet killed by an Israeli airstrike in October 2023, whose inclusion in the work was the stated reason for its cancellation. The work is performing in Venice. The state's refusal to platform it is also performing in Venice, in the empty Arsenale space beside the exhibition of the woman who built South Africa's most significant contemporary art institution.

The juxtaposition the South African state created — Kouoh's posthumous argument for what African art can do, visible to the world; Goliath's work about whose grief is permissible on a national platform, visible to the same world in an unofficial space — is a juxtaposition no editorial intelligence could have constructed with more precision. It was constructed by the state itself, through the mechanism of a ministerial letter in December and a court ruling in February that declined to engage with the constitutional arguments it was presented with.

The work is there. The sector should know it is there.

Gabrielle Goliath, Elegy. Venice, May 2026. Details via gabriellegoliath.com.

Cape Town Opera — Carmen at Artscape, 19–31 May

Artscape Theatre Centre, Cape Town  ·  19–31 May 2026

Cape Town Opera opens a new production of Bizet's Carmen at the Artscape Theatre Centre on Tuesday — the first major new opera production since Artscape marked its fifty-fifth anniversary on 19 May. The production runs through 31 May, directed and designed with a Cape Flats resetting that places the opera's formal Spanish framework into the specific social geography of the Cape — a choice that carries historical weight in a work whose central character is defined by her refusal to be possessed, her insistence on freedom as a condition she will die for rather than surrender.

Cape Town Opera deserves to be attended to as a specifically South African institutional achievement, not simply as an outpost of a European form. Founded in 2000 from the ruins of the apartheid-era CAPAB Opera, the company has spent twenty-six years building a racially integrated ensemble in a country where the apartheid state maintained separate performing-arts bodies for separate racial groups and where the opera audience, culturally and economically, was constructed to exclude the majority. The company's transformation — in its ensemble, its audience development work, its commitment to new productions that locate European operatic forms in South African realities — is one of the more sustained examples of institutional change that is structural rather than cosmetic.

The Artscape building itself frames the Carmen production with its own argument. The theatre opened in 1971 as the Nico Malan, a space built by the apartheid state explicitly for white audiences. The fifty-five years between that opening and the current season contain a specific history of what transformation looks like from inside an institution that was designed for exclusion: slow, partial, contested, and — in the current Artscape — meaningfully different from what it was. A Carmen reset in the Cape Flats, performed in a theatre that once excluded the communities from which the Cape Flats draws its culture, is the kind of layered cultural occasion that rewards attendance over description.

Cape Town Opera, Carmen. Artscape Theatre Centre, DF Malan Street, Cape Town. 19–31 May 2026. Tickets from R295 via Webtickets. capetownopera.co.za.

National Arts Festival — Makhanda, Programme Confirmed

Makhanda, Eastern Cape  ·  25 June – 5 July 2026

The National Arts Festival programme for Makhanda is now confirmed, and the main and fringe programme announcements carry sufficient signal to warrant attention ahead of the June opening. The festival runs 25 June through 5 July — eleven days in a university town in the Eastern Cape that annually becomes the most concentrated site of new South African performance work in the country.

The 2026 main programme includes, among the productions warranting specific attention: a new work from James Ngcobo's Forgotten Angle Theatre Collaborative, whose research-based practice has consistently produced some of the most formally precise South African theatre currently being made; a commission from Lesoko Seabe that engages the history of the Makhanda 1820 Settler Monument and its relationship to the town's full cultural record; and a programme of music that brings together practitioners from the Eastern Cape jazz tradition — a lineage that runs from Bra Hugh Masekela's roots through to the current generation working in a tradition the Cape Town International Jazz Festival has historically underprogrammed.

The fringe programme — traditionally the site of the most formally ambitious new work, and the place where productions that will define the next decade of South African theatre first appear — requires booking now, before the word-of-mouth that follows the opening weekend makes the most significant works unavailable. For readers who attend the festival: the fringe is the programme. The main stage confirms; the fringe discovers. That distinction has held for fifty years and holds still.

One structural observation that the 2026 festival makes available: the NAF opens in the same year that Venice demonstrated, at the largest possible scale, what South African cultural intelligence produces when given the conditions to work fully. The question the Makhanda fringe always poses — what is the work that cannot be seen anywhere else, that requires this town, this density, this specific convergence of makers and audiences — is the domestic version of the question Venice posed internationally. Both are worth holding at the same time.

National Arts Festival, Makhanda. 25 June – 5 July 2026. Main and fringe programme at artsfestival.co.za. Fringe booking open now; popular works sell out before opening weekend.

Long Essay

The Appointment: On What Zeitz MOCAA Owes Koyo Kouoh, and Why Venice Has Changed the Terms

On 9 May 2026, Koyo Kouoh's posthumous exhibition opened to the public at the Venice Biennale. She died on 10 May 2025 — 364 days earlier, one day short of a year. The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa, the institution she led from 2019 until her death, met the opening of the most significant project of her career without a named permanent successor at its head. That fact, which was notable before Venice, has a different weight now that the world has seen what the institution produces at its maximum capacity. The appointment that the Zeitz board has not yet made is now the most consequential governance decision in the South African cultural sector. What the board decides — and how it decides it — will determine whether the institution Kouoh built survives the transition, or whether it becomes, in the decade ahead, a different kind of institution wearing the same name.

The Zeitz Museum opened in 2017 in a repurposed grain silo at the V&A Waterfront in Cape Town. The building — Thomas Heatherwick's conversion of the silo's cylindrical storage chambers into galleries — received the kind of international architectural attention that can, if an institution is not careful, become its entire identity: the spectacular container that substitutes for the intellectual content it is supposed to hold. Kouoh spent the years of her leadership making sure this did not happen. Her programme decisions, her curatorial statements, her insistence on the collection as the basis for a sustained intellectual argument rather than a trophy display — all of this was organised around the same conviction: that the institution's significance lay not in its architecture but in what it thought.

What it thought, under Kouoh's leadership, was organised around a specific epistemological proposition: that African contemporary art does not require Western validation to be serious; that it operates within cosmological, philosophical, and formal traditions that are complete on their own terms; and that the institution's job is to build the infrastructure through which those traditions can be understood, argued about, and brought into productive relationship with each other and with the world. This is not a modest proposition. It is a complete reorientation of what a museum is for — from a site of collection and display to a site of argument and knowledge production. Kouoh made it the premise not of her aspiration but of her daily institutional practice.

The Venice appointment was that practice at its largest scale. The first African-born woman to curate the central exhibition of the world's most influential art event, working from an institutional home on the African continent, proposing a framework — "In Minor Keys" — that drew on jazz improvisation, Caribbean poetics, and the metaphor of the minor key as a form of expressive sovereignty outside the dominant scale. The proposition was not about African art. It was from an African curatorial position: a distinction that Kouoh understood as the difference between being the object of the world's attention and being its organising intelligence.

The succession question, in this context, is not merely a question of organisational management. It is a question about intellectual continuity. The Zeitz MOCAA board is not choosing a director — it is choosing, in effect, a position on what the institution believes it is. An appointment made for institutional safety — for a candidate who is accomplished, internationally credentialed, fluent in the language of the global art market — would produce an institution that is recognisable in the terms the global art world uses to recognise institutions. It would not necessarily produce an institution that can continue Kouoh's argument. Those are not the same thing, and the difference between them is exactly what Venice has made legible.

What does "continuing Kouoh's argument" require? Not the replication of her specific curatorial decisions — no successor should be expected to replicate a predecessor's sensibility, and a serious successor would refuse to try. What it requires is the capacity to hold the institution to the epistemological standard she set: to insist, at the level of institutional reflex rather than stated policy, that African art's formal ambition and cosmological range are not phenomena to be explained to a Western audience but propositions to be engaged on their own terms. This is a harder standard to hire for than any CV can demonstrate. It requires not just knowledge but a specific kind of confidence — the confidence to build an institution's argument from inside African intellectual traditions rather than from inside the permission structure of international art-world consensus.

The risk the board faces is the risk of institutional regression dressed as institutional continuity. The Zeitz MOCAA's collection, its building, its international profile, its Biennale history — all of these create the conditions under which an appointment can be made that maintains the surface of the institution while dismantling its substance. A director who programmes to the international art-world circuit, who manages the collection as a financial asset, who positions the institution as a gateway for African art into the global market rather than as a site for thinking about what African art knows — that director would produce an institution that looks, from the outside, like Zeitz MOCAA. It would not be.

There is a further dimension to the appointment that the board has, so far, not been required to address publicly: the relationship between the succession decision and the South African cultural sector's current governance conditions. The Zeitz MOCAA exists within a country whose public cultural infrastructure has, in the past year, demonstrated its capacity to cancel independently curated work for political reasons, to exhaust two successive attempts to organise a national Venice pavilion, and to leave the courts without a clear constitutional ruling on what section 16's protection of artistic freedom actually means in practice. The institution Kouoh built was always, among other things, a site of institutional independence — a place where the work could be uncomfortable, unresolved, and unwilling to provide reassurance, precisely because it was not subject to the ministerial oversight that governs the public institutions around it. Whether the succession decision preserves that independence, or whether the pressures of the current cultural governance environment shape the appointment toward a more comfortable, less exposed kind of institutional leadership, is a question the sector should be asking now, before the appointment is made.

None of this is to say that the transition has been badly managed. The curatorial staff who have sustained the institution since May 2025 have done so with the kind of institutional loyalty that deserves to be named as such. The programme has continued. The collection has been tended. The argument, at the level of what is on the walls, has not visibly collapsed. But continuity without a named leader is not the same as institutional succession. What the institution now needs is not the maintenance of its current state but an account of its future — a named individual who can say, publicly and in detail, what Zeitz MOCAA is for in the decade after Koyo Kouoh, and who has the intellectual authority and the institutional confidence to make that account credible.

Venice has done one thing that seventeen months of internal transition could not do on its own: it has made the stakes of the appointment visible. The world has now seen what Zeitz MOCAA produces when it operates at full capacity — when its collection and its intellectual infrastructure and its curatorial sensibility are brought to bear on the world's most visible cultural occasion. The board knows what the institution is capable of. The sector knows what is at stake. The appointment that follows Venice cannot be the same appointment that might have been made before it. That is the specific weight that 9 May 2026 has added to the governance question the board has been sitting with since 10 May 2025.

Koyo Kouoh said, in the curatorial framework she wrote in April 2025, that the exhibition would offer artists and visitors "a moment of reclaiming the museum as a place of nourishment, care, and healing" — as a space organised around the production of vitality rather than the demonstration of institutional prestige. That formulation describes not just "In Minor Keys" but the Zeitz MOCAA she ran: an institution whose prestige was a byproduct of its intellectual seriousness, not its goal. The appointment that honours that formulation will not be the safest appointment available. It will be the most specific one — the one that can name, with Kouoh's own precision, what the institution is building and why the building matters. The board's task is to find it. The sector's task is to hold the board to that standard until it does.

Closing Reflection

In Koyo Kouoh's working archive — the research, correspondence, notes, and curatorial thinking that the five advisers have been working from to realise "In Minor Keys" — there is, somewhere, an account of what she thought the institution she was leaving behind should do. She could not have known she was leaving it. She was fifty-six years old and in full work. But the archive of a curator preparing for the most significant project of her career contains, necessarily, her fullest thinking about what curatorial intelligence is for and what institutions exist to protect.

That archive is in Cape Town, in the building she transformed. The board that will appoint her successor has access to it. Whether they read it as an obligation or a legacy is the question the sector will be watching from here. One reading leads to an institution that continues. The other leads to an institution that remains.
