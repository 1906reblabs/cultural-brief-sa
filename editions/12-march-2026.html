The Cultural Brief (SA)

"It is not our differences that divide us. It is our inability to recognise, accept, and celebrate those differences." — Audre Lorde

Two productions share a bloodline this week, though neither intended it. At the Market Theatre in Newtown, Empatheatre's Isitha Sabantu — a South African reimagining of Ibsen's An Enemy of the People, drawn through the life and assassination of KwaZulu-Natal environmental defender Fikile Ntshangase — is mid-run. And last Thursday, Imraan Coovadia launched a novel titled, with full deliberateness, An Enemy of the People. Two artists, working independently in different forms, reaching toward the same Ibsen archetype in the same cultural week.

The coincidence is not merely biographical. It suggests something about the cultural temperature. The Ibsen figure — the individual who insists on a truth the community has decided it cannot afford to hear, and who is destroyed for it — keeps returning to the South African imagination. It returned in the weeks after Gabrielle Goliath's pavilion was cancelled; it returns now in theatre and in fiction simultaneously. The country keeps restaging it, as if working something out.

What it may be working out is a question that sits just beneath the surface of this week's cultural life: what does it cost, in South Africa in 2026, to tell an inconvenient truth? The costs are not always legal, as in Goliath's case. Sometimes they are physical, as in Ntshangase's. Sometimes they are quieter — the slow erosion of platform, funding, institutional support. The forms of pressure vary. The structure of the problem does not.

This week also brings the Cape Town International Jazz Festival into view — two weeks away now, but close enough to warrant considered attention. Among those on the 2026 programme is Nduduzo Makhathini, whose work asks its own question about what it means to insist, in global spaces not built to hold it, on a cosmology that belongs entirely elsewhere. This edition's long essay takes his work as its occasion.

This Week's Cultural Signal

Isitha Sabantu is running at the Market Theatre until 22 March. It asks, through puppetry, praise poetry, choral song and myth, why a grandmother in KwaZulu-Natal was shot six times in her own home for refusing a mining company's bribe. The question is not rhetorical. No one has been convicted. The play exists because the justice system has not.

Curated Dispatches

Isitha Sabantu — Market Theatre, Johannesburg (Running Until 22 March)

Empatheatre, the Durban-based company whose Isidlamlilo/The Fire Eater and The Last Country established them as one of South Africa's most rigorous makers of research-based theatre, presents the world premiere of Isitha Sabantu — Zulu for An Enemy of the People. The production received a 2024 Ibsen Scope Grant and was developed through extensive co-research with communities affected by extractivism in KwaZulu-Natal.

The work follows Nomsa, a grandmother resisting a coal mine's expansion, alongside an elephant matriarch protecting her herd — two grandmothers, one human, one not, whose fates are intertwined by the same logic of extraction. Fikile Ntshangase, who was assassinated in October 2020 after refusing to withdraw court challenges against the Tendele coal mine near Somkhele, is the shadow that falls across the play's fictional grandmother. Ntshangase's words — "I cannot sell out my people. And if need be, I will die for my people" — preceded her death by days. More than five years later, no one has been convicted.

The production blends puppetry, praise poetry, choral song, and myth, working at the intersection of ecological, spiritual and communal justice. It is the Market Theatre's 50th anniversary year; that the theatre has chosen to mark it, in part, with a work about what happens to those who refuse to be silent about land and coal, is as legible a statement as any anniversary can make.

Mannie Manim Theatre, Market Theatre, Newtown, Johannesburg. Running until 22 March. Tickets from R110 via Webtickets.

Books on the Bay — Simon's Town, This Weekend

The Books on the Bay festival is underway in Simon's Town from today through Sunday, 15 March, at the Naval Museum Dockyard Chapel, St Francis Church, the Town Hall and the Methodist Church — venues that give the weekend a gravity several registers removed from the green-room aesthetics of larger literary festivals. The 2026 line-up includes Antjie Krog, Fred Khumalo, Karen Jennings, Mandy Wiener and Don Pinnock.

Krog's presence deserves specific attention this week. Her engagement with land, language and the long arc of South African guilt and partial reckoning is precisely the kind of voice that illuminates the questions Isitha Sabantu raises from the stage. The festival's choice of historic church interiors as venues is also worth noting — there is something about sacred architecture that changes the acoustic of a conversation, slowing it, making room for weight.

Books on the Bay, Simon's Town, 13–15 March 2026. Programme and booking at booksonthebay.co.za.

Zawadi Yamungu — Ngimuhle Album Tour, Market Theatre (14 March)

On Saturday evening, Zawadi Yamungu performs her Ngimuhle Album Tour at the Market Theatre's John Kani stage — an artist celebrated for her commanding presence and her work with indigenous instruments, and for what reviewers have described as the healing potency of her voice. Yamungu draws from her heritage to bring traditional sounds into contemporary performance contexts, in a practice that shares intention — though not form — with Nduduzo Makhathini's project of returning jazz to its ancestral roots.

In a week bookended by Ibsen on one end and the approaching Cape Town International Jazz Festival on the other, Yamungu's one-night performance is the quieter, more intimate occasion — the kind of evening that serious listeners tend to remember longer than spectacle.

John Kani Theatre, Market Theatre, Newtown, Johannesburg. Saturday 14 March, doors 17:00, performance 18:00. Tickets via Webtickets.

Looking Ahead — Cape Town International Jazz Festival (27–28 March)

With two weeks remaining, the Cape Town International Jazz Festival — now in its 27th year at the CTICC — is worth previewing carefully. The 2026 international headline is Jacob Collier, the UK multi-instrumentalist whose genre-defying approach has made him one of the more genuinely surprising live performers working in jazz-adjacent music today. Alongside him: Yussef Dayes, Jasmine Myra, and, critically, Nduduzo Makhathini — whose presence on the programme connects the CTIJF to the deeper South African jazz continuum that the festival, at its best, has always understood itself to represent.

Makhathini performs An Ongoing Rehearsal: Our-Ke(s)tra with special guests — a live extension of the spiritual inquiry that has defined his Blue Note recordings. The festival's decision to programme him alongside Collier and Dayes is an interesting curatorial statement: it implies a conversation between different modes of pushing at jazz's inherited edges, some outward-facing toward pop and theory, some inward-facing toward ancestry and cosmology.

CTICC, Cape Town, 27–28 March 2026. Tickets via Ticketmaster SA.

Long Essay

Sound as Inheritance: Nduduzo Makhathini and the Cosmological Jazz

There is a question that jazz, as it migrated from its American origins into the rest of the world, has always had to answer: what happens when the music arrives somewhere with its own unresolved histories, its own spiritual architectures, its own account of what sound is for? In South Africa, that question has produced some of the most distinctive jazz of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries — Abdullah Ibrahim's township hymns, Bheki Mseleku's searching piano dialogues, Zim Ngqawana's saxophone work that seemed to conduct conversation with things not present. Each of these musicians answered the question differently. Each answer had the same direction: inward, toward a Zulu or Xhosa or Cape Malay cosmology that jazz, as a container, was made to carry even if it was not originally built for it.

Nduduzo Makhathini, born in 1982 in the semi-rural district of uMgungundlovu near Pietermaritzburg, is the inheritor and the current sharpest practitioner of this tradition. In 2020, he became the first South African artist signed to Blue Note Records — the label whose history includes Miles Davis, John Coltrane, Thelonious Monk, and Herbie Hancock. It is a fact worth noting not to frame Makhathini within that lineage, but to observe that a label which has always understood jazz as philosophy rather than entertainment recognised in him something it wanted to carry forward. His Blue Note debut, Modes of Communication: Letters from the Underworlds, was named one of the best jazz albums of 2020 by the New York Times.

But the biographical facts, while significant, are not the most interesting thing about Makhathini. The most interesting thing is the question his work poses to the very concept of musical representation — the question of what it means to carry a cosmology into spaces that were not built to hold it.

Makhathini is a practising sangoma — a traditional Zulu healer. This is not incidental to his music; it is, in his account, inseparable from it. He learned the connection between sound and healing from his grandmother, herself a sangoma. "From a very early age," he has said, "there was this very strong connection between song and some kind of healing potency and property inherent in the sound." When he encountered Coltrane's A Love Supreme in a local library, he found in it a recognition — not an introduction to something new, but a confirmation of something already known.

This is the starting point of what makes Makhathini's project distinct, and difficult to contain within the usual critical frameworks for jazz. He is not a South African musician who learned American jazz and then inflected it with local colour. He is a Zulu healer who found in jazz a vehicle adequate to a spiritual practice that predates jazz entirely. The music is not the destination; it is a mode of transport. "My work as an artist," he has said, "is to reveal messages of other worlds."

His 2024 Blue Note album uNomkhubulwane — his eleventh overall, third for the label — is the fullest expression of this to date. The title refers to a Zulu goddess: uNomkhubulwane is understood as God's daughter, a shapeshifting rain deity who manifests as animal or hurricane or rainbow, a regulator of nature and fertility. Makhathini recorded the three-movement suite in New York with bassist Zwelakhe-Duma Bell le Pere and drummer Francisco Mela, in what he has described as a process of pure metaphysical listening — allowing "supernatural voices" to direct the composition, rather than his own compositional will or the influence of his musical forebears.

There is a political dimension to this work that is easy to miss if you read it only as spiritual or aesthetic. Makhathini has consistently framed his music as a form of decolonial practice — an insistence, conducted through sound, that African cosmological knowledge is a serious epistemological system with its own rigour, its own forms of verification, its own account of what constitutes truth. When he performs at Jazz at Lincoln Center or the Philharmonie in Cologne or the Cape Town International Jazz Festival, he is not adapting African spirituality for Western consumption. He is carrying it, intact and on its own terms, into institutions whose entire architecture was built on the assumption that the knowledge worth platforming came from elsewhere.

This is a quiet form of insistence — nothing like the overt confrontation of protest art — but it is insistence nonetheless. It echoes, in its structure, the question that recurs through this week's cultural life: what does it cost to insist on a truth the room was not built to hold?

For Makhathini, the answer so far has been: not very much in the direct sense, but something in the subtler sense of sustained effort. He leads the music department at the University of KwaZulu-Natal, holds a PhD from Stellenbosch completed in 2023, and has described his entry into academic institutions as "a kind of protest" — a deliberate choice to work within systems that historically excluded African epistemological frameworks, with the explicit intention of changing them from inside. "We see more and more people in South Africa asking where are our stories in the syllables and curriculum," he has said.

The curriculum question is not a minor one. South African music education — like most South African higher education — was structured under a system that treated Western musical tradition as universal and everything else as particular, local, and therefore secondary. Makhathini's SAMA-winning album Ikhambi (2017) took its title from the Zulu word for medicinal remedy or prescription. The naming was intentional. The album was an offering — to the community, to the ancestors, to a music industry that needed a different kind of sound — and the offer was made in full public view, without apology.

It is worth considering what it means that this music exists on Blue Note — on a label founded in 1939 by two German-Jewish immigrants in New York, built around the jazz of African American musicians, and now carrying, in its catalogue, the sound of a Zulu sangoma's ancestral conversations. Jazz has always been, at root, a music made by people whose knowledge and forms of being in the world were systematically discounted by the societies around them. It became the music it is precisely because those people refused to discount themselves. Makhathini's version of that refusal is continuous with the tradition, even as its specific content — the Zulu cosmology, the sangoma practice, the uMgungundlovu hillscape — belongs entirely to a different geography.

When he performs at the CTIJF on 27 March, in the CTICC's large, efficiently managed space, he will be doing something specific: bringing into that room a practice of listening that the room's architects did not plan for. The sounds will not be alien — they will be jazz, recognisably, beautifully, rigorously. But their point of origin — the sacred space of ancestral prayer, the initiation process of ukuthwasa, the grandmother who taught a child that sound and healing are the same thing — will be present in them, whether or not the room has a category for that presence.

This is what Makhathini means when he speaks of "conditions of abundance" — a term he uses often, deliberately reclaiming it from the economic logic that has attached to it. Abundance, in his usage, means the fullness of what Africa was before the catastrophes of colonial time; it means the richness of what is still held, in memory, in practice, in sound. The music is a way of saying: it was not taken. It is still here.

That claim — understated, conducted entirely through musical means, never polemical, never defensive — is perhaps the most considered political act in South African jazz right now. It does not ask the room to change its architecture. It simply fills the room with something the architecture was not made for, and waits for the architecture to notice.

Fikile Ntshangase said, in the days before she was killed: "I cannot sell out my people. And if need be, I will die for my people." She was sixty-three. She was a former teacher. She lived near a coal mine that wanted to expand, and she was the last person between the mine and the withdrawal of the court challenges that might have stopped it.

The play about her life is running on the Market Theatre's Mannie Manim stage until 22 March. Tickets cost between R110 and R220. No one has been convicted of her murder.

The two things — the play, the impunity — sit next to each other this week without resolution. Art cannot substitute for justice. But in its absence, it is what we have.
