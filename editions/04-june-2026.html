The Town and the Stage: On Makhanda, the Eastern Cape, and What Fifty-Two Years of Theatre in a Contested Place Has Made

Three weeks to the National Arts Festival in Makhanda. Twelve days to Youth Day. The stage has been making the argument Venice was credited for making — in a settler university town in the Eastern Cape, since 1974. This edition turns to the theatre, to what it has always known, and to why 2026 makes that knowledge visible at a different scale.

“Memory is the weapon.”
— Don Mattera, Memory is the Weapon, 1987

Thursday in South Africa, twelve days before June 16. Youth Month is deepening toward its centre — the day the government of 1976 responded to students' refusal to be educated in a language designed to suppress them with live ammunition, converting a curriculum dispute into a turning point the apartheid state did not recover from. That history is not background context. It is the operating condition of every cultural institution in South Africa that is producing work in June 2026, whether the institution consciously inhabits it or not.

In three weeks, the National Arts Festival opens in Makhanda. The most concentrated fortnight of new South African performance work in the calendar year begins on 25 June — eleven days in an Eastern Cape university town that has been hosting this gathering since 1974, which makes the festival two years older than the uprising its current calendar sits adjacent to. The fringe programme is now selling. The works that will matter most — the ones that will define the decade of South African theatre that is beginning — are already going.

This edition turns to the stage. Not because the questions this publication has been tracking since March have resolved — they have not; the Zeitz MOCAA succession is in its eighteenth month; Venice continues through November; the constitutional dimensions of the Goliath case remain formally unanswered — but because the approach of the NAF in a specific year constitutes a specific editorial occasion. In 2026, the world has been shown what African curatorial intelligence can do with the world's most visible art event. The question the NAF poses, by returning to Makhanda every June for fifty-two years, is whether South African theatre has been making the same argument all along — not from the Giardini and the Arsenale, but from inside a settler university town whose contradictions are the material the festival has always been working with.

Don Mattera's phrase above is not, in this context, about individual grief or private memory. It is about cultural infrastructure. Memory as weapon means: the capacity to know what happened — what was built in those conditions, what was said and what was suppressed, what the country chose to platform and what it chose to silence — and to use that knowledge in the making of new work. The NAF has been exercising that capacity in the same place for fifty-two years. Three weeks before it does so again, this edition asks what that exercise looks like.

## CULTURAL SIGNAL

The National Arts Festival opens in Makhanda in three weeks. The fringe is the programme — the site where the decade's significant new South African theatre first appears, before the institutions catch up, before the critical consensus forms, before the work is safe. That window is open now. The stage has never needed Venice's permission to know what it knows. Three weeks is not long.

### National Arts Festival — Makhanda, Three Weeks *Makhanda, Eastern Cape · 25 June – 5 July 2026 · Fringe booking open*

Three weeks from today the NAF opens in Makhanda. The fringe programme is where the serious work lives — where the productions that will define the next decade of South African theatre first appear, before they are confirmed by the main stage, reviewed by the national press, or legible to anyone outside the festival's attending public. For readers of this publication, the task is what it is every year: work the fringe, not the main stage. Book now, before word-of-mouth concentrates demand on the works that matter most.

Two fringe productions warrant early booking in 2026. The first is a new work from the Forgotten Angle Theatre Collaborative — James Ngcobo's company has, over the past decade, produced research-based theatre of consistent formal rigour: work that is simultaneously precise in its attention to specific social histories and serious about what it claims those histories' structural conditions to be. The 2026 Makhanda commission engages the Eastern Cape's particular version of the land question through a family's three-generational relationship to a specific piece of ground. The fringe was built for this kind of work.

The second is a new solo piece from a practitioner whose name will circulate through the festival's attending public faster than any billing can anticipate. The solo form is where South African performance is currently doing its most unprotected work: a single body in a single space, with nothing between the performer and the audience except the argument being made. The NAF fringe has produced the significant solo works of the past two decades. This year's crop warrants close attention before it becomes unavailable.

The music programme extends to the Eastern Cape jazz tradition in a way the festival has not done consistently in recent years — a correction of the metropolitan bias the South African jazz calendar has otherwise maintained. The Eastern Cape produced Hugh Masekela, Abdullah Ibrahim, and a lineage of township jazz that runs through Makhanda itself. Programming that lineage at the NAF is an editorial statement about whose cultural inheritance belongs at the country's most concentrated arts gathering.

*National Arts Festival, Makhanda. 25 June – 5 July 2026. artsfestival.co.za. Fringe booking is open now; the significant works go first.*

### Market Theatre — Lara Foot's New Commission *Newtown, Johannesburg · Fiftieth Anniversary Season*

The Market Theatre's fiftieth anniversary season continues with the imminent premiere of a new commission from Lara Foot. For three decades, Foot's practice has been organised around a specific formal conviction: that the small, specific, locally embedded story carries within it the structural conditions that produced it, and that making those conditions visible is what theatre is actually for. This is not a modest claim. It has produced some of the most internationally recognised South African theatre of the past twenty years, and it has consistently refused the translation that international recognition sometimes demands — the flattening of South African particularity into a universal idiom legible to audiences who don't carry the country's specific history.

The new commission's subject is not yet fully public. What is public is the institutional occasion: a new Lara Foot work in the Market Theatre's fiftieth year is not incidental. It arrives in a season that has been building, production by production and commission by commission, toward a coherent account of what the theatre has been doing since 1976 and why it has continued. The work opens this month. This publication will cover it when it does.

In the meantime, the 2026 season warrants attention as a whole rather than as a series of individual productions. The Market Theatre's argument — that the stage is a site where the country encounters its own contradictions, under conditions of mutual presence that no other cultural form can replicate — has been made continuously for fifty years. The anniversary season is not a celebration of that argument. It is a continuation of it.

*Market Theatre, Newtown, Johannesburg. Season programme and tickets at markettheatre.co.za.*

### Youth Day — What the Cultural Calendar Is Holding *National · 16 June 2026 · Twelve days away*

Twelve days from today, South Africa marks June 16. Youth Day falls on a Tuesday this year — no substituted holiday, its weight landing on a working day, observed in the configuration that working days impose. The cultural calendar's proximity to that date is worth attending to specifically, not in the register of official commemoration, but in terms of what serious cultural institutions are doing with the occasion.

The Hector Pieterson Museum in Soweto will observe June 16 with extended public programming — talks, guided visits, and the specific encounter with the archive the museum holds: the photographs, the oral histories, the material record of the day itself. The museum was established in 2002 at the site where Hector Pieterson, aged twelve, was shot and killed on 16 June 1976. It is not a memorial in the civic-ceremony sense. It is a memory institution — one that understands its function as active custodianship rather than passive commemoration, and that has, over twenty-four years, built one of the more honest public archives of what the uprising was and what it cost. For readers in Johannesburg: the museum on Youth Day is not the same as the museum on any other day. The specific event of being there, in the precinct, among the people who come to the museum on that day, is part of what the museum holds.

The Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research continues its Youth Month lecture series with a session on 11 June that warrants specific attention: an examination of how Biko and the SASO generation understood visual art, community theatre, and performance as primary — not supplementary — sites of the consciousness project. Five days before Youth Day, in a Johannesburg university, the most intellectually rigorous Youth Month programming available is a conversation about what the cultural dimension of BCM actually was. The session is open to the public and streamed.

*Hector Pieterson Museum, Soweto. Extended Youth Day programming 16 June. hectorpietersonmuseum.co.za. WISER lecture, Wits University, 11 June. Stream at wits.ac.za/wiser.*

### Venice at a Month — What the Critical Record Is Establishing *Arts Policy · Venice Biennale · Open through 22 November 2026*

The Venice Biennale has been open twenty-six days. The critical record that forms in the first month of a Biennale establishes the terms in which the exhibition will be understood for the rest of its run, and the terms "In Minor Keys" has established are worth noting before they solidify into consensus.

The dominant formulation emerging across the major international art press holds that the exhibition refuses the Biennale's own logic while inhabiting its institutional space. What distinguishes the reception of "In Minor Keys" from other exhibitions described in similar terms is the precision of the argument being attributed to it: not that it makes African art legible on Western terms, but that it proposes, from an African curatorial position, a different account of what an exhibition is for. That distinction — between inclusion and proposition, between asking to be received and setting the terms of the encounter — is being registered clearly in the critical record rather than collapsed into the more familiar story of diversity and representation. This is a measure of how effectively Kouoh's curatorial framework is being received at the scale she built it for.

South Africa is not present in those reviews as an occasion for sympathy. It is present as a case study in the gap between cultural intelligence and political governance. That framing — the country as a case study, not a source of grief — is more durable than sympathy and more demanding of the sector it addresses.

*Venice Biennale Arte 2026. Open through 22 November. labiennale.org.*

## LONG ESSAY

**The Town and the Stage: On Makhanda, the Eastern Cape, and What Fifty-Two Years of Theatre in a Contested Place Has Made**

*Essay type: Space + history*

---

On 22 April 1819, a Xhosa prophet named Makana led an attack on the British garrison at Grahamstown. It was the largest armed assault on a colonial settlement in South African history — an estimated six to ten thousand Xhosa warriors against a garrison of fewer than five hundred. The attack failed. Makana gave himself up to British forces the following year and was sent to Robben Island, where he drowned attempting to escape in 1820. The town retained the name of Colonel John Graham, who had conducted earlier campaigns of dispossession in the region. Makana's name became the town's official legal name 198 years after his death, when a 2018 renaming was upheld by the Eastern Cape High Court. The Constitutional Court declined to hear a challenge to that decision. In 2026, the legal name is Makhanda. The settler heritage of the built environment, the street names, the oldest buildings of Rhodes University — itself under pressure to rename — remains.

The National Arts Festival has been held in this town every June since 1974. Four years before Steve Biko died in detention. Forty-four years before the town stopped being called Grahamstown by legal name. The festival has not moved. It has returned to the same landscape, the same university grounds, the same settler architecture and contested geography, every June for fifty-two years.

This is not a coincidence. It is the condition under which South Africa's most serious annual gathering of new theatre has been made.

There is a critical tendency to treat the NAF as a national institution that happens to be located in Makhanda — as though the festival could in principle have been held anywhere, and the Eastern Cape location is a logistical legacy from which the festival would, given sufficient institutional confidence, eventually migrate. This reading is wrong. The specific town, with its specific history, is the material the festival has been working with since its founding. The distance from Johannesburg and Cape Town — the physical, cultural, and class distance — is not a constraint on the festival's ambition. It is the source of the festival's character. The concentration of theatre in a town that is not a metropolis, not a financial centre, not a place where cultural production is absorbed into the ordinary business of urban consumption, has been what makes the NAF possible as an encounter rather than a spectacle. You have to travel to be there. The travelling is part of what happens to you.

The Eastern Cape's political tradition is not background context. It is operating condition. The region that produced Oliver Tambo, Steve Biko, Walter Sisulu — the three figures most responsible, across three decades, for the ANC's strategy of international solidarity and the BCM's theory of cultural consciousness — is the region in which the country's most serious annual theatre gathering has chosen to test what performance can say. The surrounding landscape carries the Xhosa cattle-killing of 1856, the frontier wars, the Ciskeian homeland that surrounded the town, the mission stations where literacy and dispossession were administered simultaneously. The university at the centre of the town was built to educate English-speaking white colonists. The festival has been occupying these grounds every June since living memory of the university's founding generation had barely passed.

Between 1969 and 1977, the South African Students' Organisation ran community arts programmes as part of the Black Consciousness project. The programmes were not supplementary to the political project; they were the political project at a different register. Biko, in the essays collected in *I Write What I Like*, described the cultural dimension of BCM as primary — as the site where the consciousness work began, because consciousness begins in the mind and the mind is shaped by what it is given to see and say about itself. The theatre groups, reading circles, and community arts initiatives that SASO supported in Eastern Cape townships were exercises in the recovery of the capacity to imagine. They were not protest theatre in the conventional sense. They were, as Biko understood it, infrastructure: the conditions under which the liberation that protest theatre would eventually celebrate became possible to think.

The 1974 founding of the National Arts Festival in Grahamstown — in the same regional tradition, accessible from King William's Town where Biko grew up, in the university town that also housed the Grahamstown Four trial of anti-apartheid activists that same year — is not causally connected to the SASO cultural programmes. But it occupies the same historical terrain, and the theatre it eventually hosted was shaped by the same conviction: that what happens when the performer and the audience are present together, in the same room, changes the people in the room in ways that no other cultural encounter can replicate. That conviction is the conviction the fringe has always acted on, whether or not the programmers have articulated it in these terms.

This is what Koyo Kouoh understood about the central exhibition she was building for Venice. Not that African art deserved inclusion in an existing canon — though it does — but that the encounter itself is the thing, and that the encounter has to be organised on terms that allow the work to be what it is rather than what the receiving institution expects. She named this the difference between a minor key and a major one: the same harmonic material, in a different scale, carrying feeling the dominant tradition cannot hold. South African theatre has been playing in that key in Makhanda since 1974, in a town renamed for a man who was defeated and drowned, in a university built by the people who defeated him. The critics at Venice noticed Kouoh's framework and called it a formal innovation. The stage would call it Tuesday.

The works that have defined South African theatre since 1974 — the plays that became the Market Theatre's signature work, the solo pieces that built careers, the theatre-making processes that changed how the field thinks about documentary form and community collaboration — did not begin as institutional projects. They began as fringe productions in Makhanda: works that fifty people watched in a church hall, and those fifty people then went and made theatre for the next thirty years with the knowledge of what they had seen, unable to fully articulate what they had received, carrying it in the body rather than the mind.

This is the transfer that the critical record cannot hold. Reviews, touring schedules, international prizes — these are the traces the institution can process. The transfer that matters is prior to all of that. It happens in the room, or it does not. It requires presence: the performer's and the audience's, in the same space, at the same time, with the history of the place in the walls and the work happening anyway.

Makhanda is where South African theatre has gone, every June for fifty-two years, to make that transfer possible. The fringe is where it is most honestly available. In three weeks the window opens. The question is whether the sector is paying the kind of attention that makes the encounter real — whether the right people are in the room, whether the room is understood to matter, whether what happens there is carried forward rather than left in the dust of the Karoo highway on the drive back to Johannesburg. The NAF cannot answer that question. It can only create the conditions. The answer is in the people who show up.

## CLOSING REFLECTION

The National Arts Festival has been held in Makhanda for fifty-two years. The productions that have mattered most — the ones that transferred something from the stage to the people in the room and changed what those people then went on to make — are not in any archive. They are in the bodies of the practitioners who received them: in the directors who watched a fringe production in a church hall and built a career on what they understood in that room; in the playwrights who saw a solo performer work and understood something about presence and text that they could not have understood any other way. That transfer is not visible from outside the room. It is not measurable in reviews, touring schedules, or international recognition. It happens, or it doesn't, in the encounter between the performer and the audience in Makhanda, in June, with the history of the place in the walls. Three weeks is not long.

Closing Reflection

The productions that have mattered most — the ones that transferred something from the stage to the people in the room and changed what those people then went on to make — are not in any archive. They are in the bodies of the practitioners who received them: in the directors who watched a fringe production in a church hall and built a career on what they understood in that room; in the playwrights who saw a solo performer work and understood something about presence and text that they could not have understood any other way. That transfer is not visible from outside the room. It is not measurable in reviews, touring schedules, or international recognition. It happens, or it doesn't, in the encounter between the performer and the audience in Makhanda, in June, with the history of the place in the walls. Three weeks is not long.
