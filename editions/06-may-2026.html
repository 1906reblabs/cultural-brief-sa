The Music, Now That It Is Playing: On What "In Minor Keys" Shows South Africa About Itself

Today the Venice Biennale preview opens. For the first time in the history of the world's most influential art exhibition, the central show was conceived by an African woman working from an institutional home on the African continent. She is not here to see it. South Africa's pavilion space beside it is empty. May is Africa Month. In the Wits School of Art in Braamfontein, a piano festival opens on Friday. The question this edition carries is not what was promised. It is what was made — and what the country it was made from has done with the gift.

"In our myths, in our songs, that's where the seeds are. It is not possible to constantly hold onto crisis. You have to have the love and you have to have the magic, that's also life."
— Toni Morrison, interview, 1977. Cited by Koyo Kouoh in the curatorial framework of In Minor Keys.

Wednesday in South Africa, and in Venice. Today the 61st International Art Exhibition of La Biennale di Venezia opens its preview days — three days for the press and the sector before the public opening on Saturday, 9 May. What is opening, in the Giardini and the Arsenale and across the city's palazzi and courtyards and canalside spaces, is a vision that was conceived entirely before May 10, 2025: the day Koyo Kouoh died.

The scale of what she completed is only now becoming clear. By the time she died, she had already defined the theoretical framework of the exhibition, selected all 111 participating artists, begun selecting the specific works with them, determined the graphic identity, commissioned the catalogue, and appointed the architecture of the exhibition. The curatorial team she chose — Gabe Beckhurst Feijoo, Marie Hélène Pereira, Rasha Salti as advisors; Siddhartha Mitter as editor-in-chief; Rory Tsapayi as research assistant — has realised her vision from that archive. The last major working session she convened was in Dakar, in April 2025, at the RAW Material Company she founded. The team has described it as the moment when they could "hear the music Koyo had composed." A month later she was dead. The music opens today.

This edition is the first to be published while "In Minor Keys" is visible. Everything this publication has written about Kouoh and Venice over the past three months has been written in anticipation of what would be produced. We now know. 111 artists from across the globe, selected for their affinities and possible convergences; processions and oases and schools and shrines as the spatial metaphors organising the experience; Issa Samb and Beverly Buchanan at the heart of the Central Pavilion as an opening gesture of what the exhibition understands art to be; a deliberate refusal of the encyclopedic and the didactic in favour of the atmospheric and the sensory. The world is in Venice this week to encounter what an African curatorial intelligence understood the world's most visible art event to be for. South Africa is not there to represent itself beside it.

That absence is not separate from the opening. It is part of what the opening illuminates. The country whose most significant cultural institution nurtured Kouoh's final years — whose government cancelled the independent curatorial selection twice and exhausted both processes before the Biennale opened — will be named this week in every serious piece of writing about "In Minor Keys," not because of what it contributed, but because of what it withheld. This is the condition in which this Wednesday arrives. Africa Month has begun. The Wits Standard Bank Piano Festival opens on Friday in Braamfontein, continuing a tradition of South African musical thought that connects to the same cosmological currents Kouoh invoked in building her exhibition. These things are happening. They are happening in a country that, for the third month running, cannot answer the question the opening of Venice is posing to it.

"In Minor Keys" is open. The music Koyo Kouoh composed is now audible to the world. South Africa's pavilion space in the Arsenale is empty. These are not separate facts. They are the same fact, expressed in two different registers — one of what a country's cultural intelligence can produce when it is given the conditions to work, and one of what happens when the state that houses that intelligence decides its own comfort matters more than the work.

Curated Dispatches

"In Minor Keys" — The Venice Biennale Opens

Venice  ·  Preview 6–8 May  ·  Public Opening 9 May–22 November 2026

The 61st International Art Exhibition opens its preview today. What the world is encountering in Venice from this Wednesday is the fullest realisation yet of what Koyo Kouoh spent her career arguing: that African curatorial intelligence, working from African intellectual traditions and on its own epistemological terms, has something to say to the entire world — not as supplement to a Western art-historical framework but as its own complete proposition.

The exhibition's 111 participating artists were selected, Kouoh wrote, for their "resonances, affinity, and possible convergences" rather than for their geographic representation or their institutional recognition. The curatorial framework draws on jazz improvisation, Caribbean poetics, and the metaphor of the Creole garden — on practices of making beauty collectively, of working across forms and frequencies, of refusing the accelerated productivity logic that the Biennale's institutional scale normally demands. The motifs Kouoh identified — processions, oases, schools, shrines — organise the physical experience of the exhibition not as thematic sections but as spatial atmospheres. The visitor is not directed through an argument; they are invited to tune in.

At the heart of the Central Pavilion, Kouoh placed a homage to Issa Samb and Beverly Buchanan — two artists who worked, as Kouoh did, outside monumental logic, prioritising processes and community over objects and permanence. The gesture is programmatic: it announces, from the first moment of encounter, what this exhibition understands art to be. Not a spectacle of accumulated cultural capital, but a generative energy. Not objects to be consumed, but relationships to be entered.

South Africa is absent from the 100 national participations. The Arsenale pavilion space that should carry a South African voice carries nothing. Gabrielle Goliath's Elegy — the work that was selected, cancelled, re-selected, and abandoned through two failed processes — will be shown in an alternative Venice space. The work exists. The state's capacity to represent itself beside it does not.

The full weight of what this opening means for South African cultural life will take time to absorb. The long essay this week attempts a first reckoning.

Venice Biennale Arte 2026. Preview 6–8 May. Public opening 9 May, running to 22 November. Giardini and Arsenale, Venice. labiennale.org.

Wits Standard Bank Piano Festival — Braamfontein, This Weekend

Wits School of Art, Braamfontein, Johannesburg  ·  8–10 May 2026

On Friday evening, in the Chris Seabrooke Music Hall at the Wits School of Art in Braamfontein, the Wits Standard Bank Piano Festival begins its second edition. Eight pianists across four double-bill concert evenings: Thembi Dunjana and Thandi Ntuli on Friday, Yonela Mnana and Bokani Dyer on Saturday afternoon, Hilton Schilder and Andile Yenana on Saturday evening, Megan Geoffrey Prins and Taissa Poliakova on Sunday afternoon.

The lineup is a cross-section of South African piano practice that resists easy categorisation. Dunjana and Ntuli work in registers where jazz, composition, and African musical lineage are not separate territories. Bokani Dyer's practice extends from improvisation to orchestral work; his 2019 album Radio Sello remains among the most formally ambitious South African piano records of the past decade. Hilton Schilder brings decades of Cape jazz idiom — a tradition rooted in specific Cape community histories — into a concert programme alongside Andile Yenana, whose own practice navigates the formal demands of the Western classical tradition from inside a distinctly South African set of references. Megan Geoffrey Prins and Taissa Poliakova close the festival with a double bill that holds South African and Russian-heritage classical practice in the same space, without hierarchy.

The festival is worth attending this specific weekend for a specific reason: it places in a Johannesburg concert hall, on the same weekend that Venice opens Koyo Kouoh's posthumous argument about African artistic intelligence, a series of South African practitioners whose work makes that argument in sound. The connection is not editorial. It is structural. The music Kouoh cited — jazz, improvisation, the minor key as a form of expressive sovereignty — is being made, this Friday and Saturday and Sunday, two kilometres from where Kouoh herself worked when she was in Johannesburg.

Wits Standard Bank Piano Festival. Chris Seabrooke Music Hall, Wits School of Art, Braamfontein. Friday 8 May at 7pm; Saturday 10 May at 3pm and 7pm; Sunday 10 May at 3pm. Tickets from R300 per evening via Webtickets.

Artscape at Fifty-Five — Cape Town, 19 May

Cape Town  ·  19 May 2026  ·  Advance Notice

On 19 May, the Artscape Theatre Centre in Cape Town marks fifty-five years of operation. The anniversary is held under the theme "A Place for All" — a framing that carries more historical weight than it might appear. The building opened in 1971 as the Nico Malan Theatre, a space built under apartheid specifically for white audiences, with a legislative exclusion of Black South Africans from its performances and its foyers that was not merely a matter of social custom but of law.

The transformation of the institution — from a space defined by exclusion to one that now programmes community groups, school bands, contemporary dance, opera, and jazz with explicit commitments to access and representation — is a story that is worth examining more carefully than anniversary events typically permit. Marlene Le Roux, the institution's executive director, has described accessibility not as a concept but as "a daily commitment" that includes physical access, representational programming, and ensuring that people feel they belong in the space. Whether the institution has closed the gap between that commitment and its practice is a question the anniversary programme itself will begin to answer.

What the 19 May celebration launches is not a single performance but a programme: a memory wall unveiling titled Becoming Artscape — A Place Reborn, an open press conference, a historic photograph on the building's steps, and a continuation of a season that now includes Cape Town Opera's new production of Carmen (19–31 May). The institutional story is more interesting than the individual events within it. A building once built to exclude hosting an anniversary under the theme of inclusion — that arc, across fifty-five years, is the editorial subject. Whether the content of the programming matches the aspiration of the framing is what the coming weeks will show.

Artscape Theatre Centre, DF Malan Street, Cape Town. Anniversary events from 11:00 on 19 May. artscape.co.za. Cape Town Opera's Carmen runs 19–31 May. Tickets via Webtickets.

Africa Month — What the Occasion Asks This Year

National  ·  May 2026  ·  Africa Day 25 May

May is Africa Month in South Africa — the country's annual commemoration of the founding of the Organisation of African Unity on 25 May 1963, now observed as Africa Day. The Department of Sport, Arts and Culture programmes events through the month; the occasion is, in its official form, a celebration of continental unity, of shared identity, of the aspiration toward an Africa that can speak for itself on the world stage.

This year, Africa Month arrives with an unusual specificity. The Venice Biennale — the world's most influential art event — has opened under the first curatorial vision ever conceived by an African-born woman working from an institutional home on the African continent. The 63 years since the OAU was founded are, among other things, 63 years of African intellectuals and artists building the infrastructure for a different kind of relationship between the continent and the global cultural order: not supplication, not assimilation, but the construction of the terms on which African thought enters the room. "In Minor Keys" is that project at its most visible scale. It opens during Africa Month. The coincidence is not coincidence — it is what 63 years of cultural infrastructure-building produces when the work is done with sufficient seriousness.

The official Africa Month programming — government events, departmental celebrations, the Africa Day ceremony — will carry its customary register of commemoration and aspiration. What is worth attending to this May, alongside the official occasions, is what African artistic and intellectual practice is actually doing: in Venice, in Braamfontein, in the theatres and galleries and studios of a continent whose cultural intelligence has been building, through the years the continent was being theorised as a problem, toward the condition that Kouoh named in 2025 as the curatorial frame for the world's attention. The time, as she wrote in her own poem, has come.

Long Essay

The Music, Now That It Is Playing: On What "In Minor Keys" Reveals, and What South Africa Cannot Yet Hear

In April 2025, Koyo Kouoh convened her curatorial team at RAW Material Company in Dakar for a week of collective work on the exhibition she had been building since accepting the Venice appointment the previous October. The meeting was, in retrospect, the last full gathering before her death the following month. Those who were there have described it as the moment when they could "hear the music Koyo had composed" — a week in which the exhibition's themes and its emotional logic became audible as a whole rather than visible as separate decisions. She died eleven days before she was due to present it to the world. Today, in Venice, the world is hearing it for the first time.

"In Minor Keys" is not an exhibition about African art. This distinction is essential, and it is the distinction that most coverage of the Biennale has struggled to hold. It is an exhibition that proceeds from an African curatorial intelligence — from a mind formed in Bamenda, trained in Europe, built into institutional significance through Dakar and then Cape Town — but its 111 participating artists come from many geographies, selected for what Kouoh called "resonances, affinity, and possible convergences between practices, even when far apart." The exhibition does not gather African artists to represent Africa to the West. It proposes, from an African curatorial position, a different account of what art is for and what a Biennale can be.

The account draws on jazz as its primary metaphor, and the metaphor is doing serious philosophical work. Jazz is a music built on the productive tension between structure and freedom, between the given harmonic material and what the individual musician does with it, between the ensemble's shared premises and each player's sovereign improvisation. The minor key — the flattened third, the emotional register that the major scale cannot hold — is jazz's preferred territory not because it is sad but because it is honest: it carries feeling the more comfortable key refuses. Kouoh's proposition, made in the framework she sent to the Biennale's president on 8 April 2025, is that the exhibition will operate like a free-jazz ensemble: practices brought together for their "common premise" rather than their common geography, creating "a scramble of cohesion and dissonance" rather than the encyclopedic or the didactic.

What this produces, in physical space, is something the world's most visited art exhibition has not offered before: an experience organised around atmosphere rather than argument, around sensation rather than signification, around what Kouoh described as "the emotional, the visual, the sensory, the affective, the subjective." The motifs she identified — processions, oases, schools, shrines — are not curatorial categories but spatial registers. The procession invites participation, movement, the experience of being carried. The oasis offers pause, repair, the recovery of something that accelerated attention has depleted. The school proposes exchange and social responsibility as artistic conditions. The shrine acknowledges what exceeds institutional framing entirely.

At the heart of the Central Pavilion, in the Sala Chini, Kouoh placed a homage to Issa Samb and Beverly Buchanan — two figures who worked, throughout their careers, outside monumental logic. Samb, the Senegalese artist and philosopher, made practice inseparable from communal life and refused the objecthood that the Western art market demanded. Buchanan, the American sculptor and activist, built from found and discarded materials in conditions of radical economic constraint, producing work that the art world's institutional machinery consistently undervalued and that is only now being understood at its full significance. The choice to open the exhibition with these two is a statement about what the exhibition honours: not the art that succeeded within the terms the dominant institutions established, but the art that succeeded on different terms entirely — that built its significance in the refusal of those terms.

South Africa should be in Venice beside this. Not because "In Minor Keys" is a South African exhibition — it is not, and to claim it as such would be precisely the territorial logic Kouoh was resisting — but because the country that housed her most significant institutional work, and that produced the conditions in which the world's largest dedicated collection of African contemporary art was built, had the opportunity to stand at the centre of the most significant art event of this century's third decade and say something on its own behalf. The selection committee twice chose Gabrielle Goliath's Elegy to say it. The state twice refused to let it be said. The pavilion is empty.

What the emptiness costs cannot be easily quantified. There is the immediate cost: the Biennale is attended by the collectors, curators, critics, and institutional decision-makers who shape the global art world's year. South African artists and institutions seeking international recognition will navigate that world for the next eighteen months without the signal that pavilion presence sends. There is the medium-term cost: the precedent that ministerial content veto has been established without legal reversal, and that future ministers now know this mechanism is available to them. And there is the structural cost, which is harder to name and more significant: the demonstration that the state's relationship to its own cultural intelligence is extractive rather than supportive — that it will accept the prestige generated by institutions like Zeitz MOCAA while refusing the conditions under which that prestige was produced.

Koyo Kouoh was explicit about what those conditions required. She said in multiple interviews and institutional statements that the museum's function was to create the conditions under which art could do its most complete work — which meant protecting the space in which work could be uncomfortable, unresolved, and unwilling to provide reassurance. She said this about Zeitz MOCAA. She was, in everything she did, saying it about the entire ecosystem of South African cultural production. The curatorial intelligence she brought to Venice was not separable from the institutional intelligence she brought to Cape Town: both were organised around the same conviction that the infrastructure for African art to speak on its own terms must be built, protected, and used without apology.

The institution she built is now in its sixteenth month without a named permanent successor. The exhibition she curated opens today without her. The country she called home for the last years of her life has sent no one to stand beside the result of her work. The Zeitz MOCAA collection she assembled — the largest dedicated collection of African contemporary art in the world — continues to be held and shown in the building she transformed, by the staff she trained, in the intellectual culture she created. What happens to that culture in the absence of the person who held it together is the most consequential question in the South African cultural sector right now. It does not have an answer yet. The institution's silence about its own future is itself an answer of a kind.

What "In Minor Keys" reveals about South Africa is not what it shows — it does not show South Africa, not directly, not in the way a national pavilion would. What it reveals is the gap between what the country's cultural intelligence is capable of producing, when given the conditions to produce it, and what the country's governing institutions are capable of protecting. Kouoh produced something that the world's most serious art critics will be examining and debating until November. The state produced an empty room. That gap is not a gap between aspiration and delivery. It is a gap between what South Africa is and what it claims to be — and it is the gap that this publication, and this sector, and this country's serious cultural readers, have the obligation to continue naming until the naming produces something different from what it has so far produced.

Toni Morrison wrote, in 1977, that it is not possible to constantly hold onto crisis. You have to have the love and you have to have the magic. Kouoh cited her. The citation matters: it names what "In Minor Keys" is trying to do and what the South African state, in cancelling Elegy, was refusing to permit. Elegy is a work of mourning. Mourning is love for the dead. The state decided it was divisive. The exhibition that opens today in Venice — the exhibition that is, at its core, an argument for the radicality of joy, for the nourishment of what Kouoh called art's "natural habitat" — was conceived by a woman who understood mourning and love as the same practice, in different keys. She is gone. The music plays.

Closing Reflection

Koyo Kouoh wrote a poem. She dated it 2020, during the first year of the pandemic, and it was read at the Biennale's announcement ceremony in May 2025, six days after her death. It ends: "We are all tired. The world is tired, even art itself is tired. We need the radicality of joy. The time has come."

She did not write that as an epitaph. She wrote it as a curatorial argument — as the emotional logic underlying what she was building for Venice. It is an argument the South African state refused to platform, in January, when it cancelled the selection. The refusal is now inscribed in the absence of a South African voice beside the exhibition that argument is opening. The time had come. South Africa was not there when it did.
