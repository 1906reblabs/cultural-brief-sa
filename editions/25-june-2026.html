What the Fringe Holds: On the National Arts Festival Opening, the Freedom That Comes Before Permission, and What 2026 Made

The National Arts Festival opens today in Makhanda. Four editions of advance notice have reached their occasion. The fringe — where the decade's significant new South African theatre first becomes visible — begins this morning in the Eastern Cape university town the festival has returned to every June since 1974. Venice is forty-seven days in. The Zeitz MOCAA succession is in its nineteenth month without a public resolution. Youth Day was nine days ago. This is the first edition published on the opening day.

“The most authentic thing about us is our capacity to create, to overcome, to endure, to transform, to love and to be greater than our suffering.”
— Ben Okri, A Way of Being Free, 1997

Thursday in South Africa, and the National Arts Festival opens this morning in Makhanda. Four editions of advance notice have reached their occasion. The fringe — three hundred productions across eleven days, in church halls and converted spaces and university studios throughout an Eastern Cape university town — begins today. This publication has been naming it for four weeks as the site where the decade's significant new South African theatre first becomes visible. Today it opens.

The fringe's founding proposition is that work enters the world before the institution has decided what it is — before the reviews, before the consensus, before the main stage signals that it is safe to attend. What opens today in a Makhanda church hall or university studio is not aspiring to the main stage's confirmation. It is exercising the prior claim: that the work has the right to exist, and to be encountered, before anyone has determined that it deserves to be. Three hundred productions across eleven days. Not all of them consequential. Not all of them even competent. The fringe has never guaranteed quality. It has guaranteed the encounter.

The 2026 fringe carries specific weight. The works on stage today were made in a year in which the 50th anniversary of the Soweto uprising was the approaching occasion, in which Venice was demonstrating at the largest possible scale what South African curatorial intelligence produces when it works from its own premises, in which the Zeitz MOCAA board's nineteen-month silence was accumulating meaning that the board had not chosen to articulate publicly. Those conditions are in the work whether the programmes name them or not. The fringe has never required its productions to be explicitly topical. It requires only that the work be made from the conditions in which the maker lives, and those conditions include the year, and 2026 was a specific year.

The distinction between the fringe and the main stage is structural, not evaluative. The main stage confirms what institutions have already decided matters — it platforms work that has been assessed, selected, resourced, and positioned within an institutional argument. The fringe encounters work before the apparatus has caught up with it. This is not a romantic claim about authenticity. It is an observation about where the risk lives, and therefore where the discovery lives. The main stage is where the known becomes visible. The fringe is where the not yet known becomes possible to see.

The Zeitz MOCAA enters its nineteenth month without a named director. Venice is forty-seven days in, its critical record now stable. Youth Day was nine days ago. These are not the fringe's concerns in any direct institutional sense — they are the conditions in which the fringe's 2026 work was made. When a production engages what the country does with what its dead made possible, or what institutional silence produces in the space it leaves, or how a community absorbs a year that has been watching it — and some will — the Zeitz's vacancy and Venice's reception are in the room even if neither is named.

The essay this week holds a different argument. Section 16(1)(c) of the South African Constitution protects freedom of artistic creativity. The Goliath case in March 2026 offered the courts an opportunity to engage what that protection means when the state is the infringer. Judge Mamoloko Kubushi dismissed on contractual grounds, without written reasons, leaving the constitutional question formally unanswered. Today, in Makhanda, three hundred productions are exercising the same right the Goliath case declined to adjudicate — not through litigation, but through performance. The essay asks what the difference is, and why it matters for how the sector understands the freedom it has been exercising for fifty-two years without requiring a court to confirm it.

The National Arts Festival opens today. The work made in 2026 — when the 50th anniversary was approaching, when Venice was demonstrating what South African curatorial intelligence produces, when the Zeitz board's silence was accumulating meaning — is on stage now. Not all of it will carry the weight. The fringe never guarantees that. It only guarantees the encounter.

Curated Dispatches

The Fringe Opens Today

National Arts Festival  ·  Makhanda, Eastern Cape  ·  25 June – 5 July 2026

The National Arts Festival opens this morning. The fringe — three hundred productions across eleven days, in church halls, university studios, and converted spaces throughout Makhanda — is the site this publication has been naming for four editions as the place where the decade's significant new South African theatre first becomes visible. Today it opens.

The opening weekend is the most important moment in the festival's calendar, not because it contains the best work but because it generates the word-of-mouth that determines what is still available by the festival's final weekend. The critical consensus on which productions matter typically forms across the first three or four days. Being in Makhanda for the opening weekend, available to the word-of-mouth as it circulates, is a different attendance to arriving with the consensus already formed. The works that carry the 2026 fringe's weight are being confirmed today.

The solo performance strand deserves specific attention. It is where the fringe has consistently produced the most formally ambitious work of each decade — a single body in a single space, with nothing between the performer and the audience except the argument being made. The 2026 fringe's solo programme was made in the year the 50th anniversary was the approaching occasion. That operating context is in the work's structure. The institution decides what the main stage carries. The fringe carries what the maker could not not make.

National Arts Festival, Makhanda. 25 June – 5 July 2026. artsfestival.co.za. Book early; the significant works go first.

The Lineage in the Room

Eastern Cape Jazz Programme  ·  Makhanda  ·  NAF 2026

The Eastern Cape jazz tradition runs from the mission-station musical culture of the nineteenth century through the township communities that produced Abdullah Ibrahim, Hugh Masekela, and Kippie Moeketsi, and carries within it a cosmological understanding of sound — a relationship between music, communal identity, and what the Xhosa and Sotho traditions understand as the proper relationship between the living and the inherited — that the dominant Western jazz idiom does not contain and cannot translate. It absorbed American jazz as one of many influences and remained something else: a parallel tradition with its own epistemological content, its own criteria for what music is for.

The NAF jazz programme's 2026 Eastern Cape lineage strand restores a curatorial emphasis the festival has not sustained consistently across recent years. Programming this tradition at the NAF is not a compensatory gesture. It is a statement about what the festival understands itself to be for: not a gateway between African musical practice and the international circuits that would assess it, but a site at which African music encounters its own inherited complexity, on its own terms, in the region that produced the specific lineage being performed.

This is the same argument Koyo Kouoh made about Venice — that the encounter with the work should be organised on the work's own terms, not on the receiving institution's. The jazz programme makes that argument without calling it a curatorial framework. It does so by programming what the lineage requires, in the place the lineage comes from.

National Arts Festival jazz programme, Makhanda. 25 June – 5 July 2026. artsfestival.co.za.

Nineteen Months, and the Post-Anniversary Silence

Zeitz MOCAA  ·  Cape Town  ·  V&A Waterfront  ·  Institutional governance

The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa entered its nineteenth month without a named permanent director in the week after South Africa marked fifty years of June 16. The anniversary passed. The NAF now opens. The institution, which holds the largest dedicated collection of African contemporary art in the world, did not visibly engage either occasion with the curatorial weight its collection, its institutional history, and its stated intellectual mission would have positioned it to bring.

This publication has been tracking the succession void since it became a sustained institutional condition. What distinguishes the post-anniversary moment is the specificity of the accumulated silences. June 16, 2026 — the fiftieth anniversary of the uprising that produced the political-cultural consciousness tradition Koyo Kouoh spent her career building institutional infrastructure for — passed without a prominent publicly visible curatorial response from the institution most directly positioned to make one. The NAF opens today. The Zeitz is not institutionally present to either occasion in any way the sector can assess.

The succession question and the institutional silences are not separable. An institution in its nineteenth month without a director is not an institution that can respond to curatorial occasions at the scale those occasions require. These silences are now measured not against hypothetical occasions but against specific ones that have passed without institutional response.

Zeitz MOCAA, V&A Waterfront, Cape Town. Open Tuesday–Sunday. zeitzmocaa.museum.

The Record Settles

Venice Biennale  ·  Arts Policy  ·  Open through 22 November 2026

The Venice Biennale has been open forty-seven days. The critical record is now stable. In Minor Keys — Koyo Kouoh's posthumous central exhibition, executed by five curatorial advisers working from her archive — has been received not as a statement about diversity and representation but as a formal argument about what an exhibition is for. The advisers did not interpret Kouoh's framework. They executed it. That distinction is visible in the critical record, and it is what makes the exhibition a measure rather than a memorial.

The South African position in that record is not sympathetic. It is analytical. The country that produced the curator who made the argument, and then cancelled the independent selection process twice and left its pavilion space empty while the posthumous exhibition opened beside it, occupies a specific and unambiguous position in the documentation of this Biennale. The position was established in the first month. It will not change before November. No subsequent DSAC communication will alter what the record already holds.

The NAF opens today in Makhanda, and the fringe will carry some of what Venice received. The sector that missed Venice is present in Makhanda. That is not equivalence. It is sequence.

Venice Biennale Arte 2026. Open through 22 November 2026. labiennale.org.

Long Essay

Before Permission: On the NAF Fringe, the Goliath Affair, and What Artistic Freedom Actually Requires

Section 16(1)(c) of the South African Constitution protects freedom of artistic creativity as a fundamental right. It was included in the Bill of Rights in 1996 in direct response to the apartheid state's machinery of cultural censorship — the Publications Control Board, the proscription lists, the banning orders that had suppressed not only political speech but the aesthetic traditions through which South African artists had been arguing, for decades, that the country in which they lived was not the country the state described. The right was hard-won, deliberately framed, and understood by those who drafted it as one of the preconditions for the cultural life the new democracy was supposed to make possible. Thirty years later, the courts had not significantly tested it. That changed in 2026, and the test revealed less about the right's scope than about the infrastructure required to make it real.

The Goliath affair was, at its constitutional centre, a question about ministerial content interference in independently curated state-sponsored art. Liesl Kotzé's independent selection process had selected Daniella Mooney's performance work Elegy to represent South Africa at the Venice Biennale. The then-Minister of Sport, Arts and Culture intervened in that selection, citing content characterised as inappropriate for a national cultural representation. The selection was cancelled — twice. Mooney ultimately presented Elegy at Venice in an alternative space, outside the official national pavilion, to critical reception that observers described as among the most significant performances of the Biennale. The pavilion stood empty. The minister attended the Biennale. The work was there without the state's platform, which is to say the work was there on its own terms, which is the condition section 16 was designed to make available.

Justice Mamoloko Kubushi of the Gauteng High Court dismissed the case in March 2026 on contractual grounds. The contract between the Department of Sport, Arts and Culture and the commissioner did not contain explicit protections against ministerial content interference. The court found the contractual terms dispositive and did not reach the constitutional questions. The judgment was issued without written reasons. The constitutional question — whether section 16 protects an independently selected work from ministerial veto when the state is the exhibition's funder — was left formally unanswered. This is not a minor procedural omission. The absence of reasons is itself a governance failure: a court that declines to explain why it dismissed a constitutional argument on threshold grounds is a court that has declined to engage the constitution's reach in the conditions in which the reach matters most.

What the Goliath case revealed is the gap between the right's existence and the infrastructure required to make it enforceable. Section 16(1)(c) exists. It is unambiguous. It was deliberately included in the Bill of Rights. But a right without an adequate governance structure to enforce it is a statement of principle, not a protection. The contract did not constrain the minister. The court did not find the contractual lacuna to be a governance failure. The constitutional question was never reached. At every stage, the infrastructure was inadequate to the right it was supposed to protect — and the inadequacy was not discovered by the case but confirmed by it.

Today, the NAF fringe opens in Makhanda with approximately three hundred productions. No minister approved them. No department signed off on their content. The producers hold lease agreements for performance spaces and contracts with performers. No mechanism exists by which a minister could cancel a fringe production — not because such cancellation would be unconstitutional, though it would, but because the governance structure of the fringe does not contain a single point of ministerial authority. This structural fact is not a deliberate design decision against state interference. It is a consequence of the fringe's distributed architecture: hundreds of producers, hundreds of spaces, the festival serving as coordinator rather than gatekeeper. The fringe is not protected from ministerial interference by the constitution. It is protected by structure.

Daniella Mooney said, after the pavilion cancellation, that the work is not contingent on any particular platform. This is the correct statement of what section 16 protects: not the right to a specific platform, but the prior right to make the work — the right that is logically antecedent to any question of where the work is exhibited or by whom. Elegy was made. It was performed. The pavilion's emptiness did not prevent the work from existing or from being encountered. But Mooney's observation, while constitutionally accurate, describes the minimum — the floor that section 16 guarantees rather than the ceiling it was designed to reach. What section 16 was designed to protect is not simply the right to make the work somewhere. It is the right to have the work encountered in the conditions that make the encounter possible — not in alternative spaces that the maker's resilience was required to find, but in the infrastructure the state holds in trust for the cultural life the democracy was supposed to produce.

The fringe operates below this threshold by design. Fringe producers do not expect state-provided pavilions. They expect only that the spaces they have contracted will be available, and that their productions will be permitted to run. The state's role in the fringe is minimal, and the fringe's resilience is in part a consequence of that minimalism. But this resilience should not be misread as a model for all South African cultural production. The fringe's distributed structure is available to certain kinds of work, made under certain conditions, produced at certain costs. The Venice Biennale pavilion is a different kind of institutional occasion — one that requires state resources, state representation, and state infrastructure. Section 16 was designed to protect cultural production in both registers. The Goliath case revealed that the infrastructure to protect it in the second register does not currently exist.

What the infrastructure requires is not complex in principle. It requires explicit contract terms that constrain ministerial interference in independently selected state-sponsored cultural representations. It requires governance structures for institutions like the South African Pavilion that specify the conditions under which ministerial content review is and is not permissible — and that make those limits legally enforceable rather than aspirationally stated. It requires arts funding legislation that builds the institutional independence of publicly funded cultural organisations into the legal framework rather than leaving it to the goodwill of successive ministers. These are not novel propositions. They are the standard governance conditions under which comparable institutions in comparable democracies operate. South Africa has the constitutional right. It has not built the governance infrastructure the right requires to be real.

It also requires courts that are willing to engage section 16 when the question is brought before them — courts that understand a contractual dismissal without written reasons as the constitutional failure it is, and that are prepared to identify governance lacunae as themselves inconsistent with the right the constitution protects. The Goliath case was dismissed without reasons. The constitutional question was not reached. The absence of reasons is a record. When the question is next brought — and it will be brought again, because the conditions that produced the Goliath affair have not changed — the record includes not only the ministerial cancellation but the court's silence about what the constitution requires of a state that holds cultural infrastructure in trust for the citizens whose freedom it is supposed to protect.

The fringe opens today. Three hundred productions are exercising section 16(1)(c) without invoking it, without knowing they are invoking it, without requiring a court to adjudicate what the right protects. The encounter between the performer and the audience, in the church halls and converted spaces of Makhanda, is what the right was designed to make possible. The fringe has always known this, not as constitutional theory but as operational practice: the work is made, the space is contracted, the audience arrives, the encounter happens. The right was there before the constitution named it. The fringe was there before the right was codified. The court that declined to engage section 16's scope in the Goliath case left a constitutional question unanswered. The fringe opened this morning without waiting for the answer.

Closing Reflection

The Goliath affair left one constitutional question formally unanswered: what does section 16 protect when the state itself is the infringer? The court gave no reasons. The question stands. In Makhanda, today, three hundred productions are making the same claim Elegy made in Venice: that the right to make the work is prior to the institution's decision to platform it, and that what happens in the room when performer and audience encounter each other is what the right exists to protect. The encounter is not the same in every room. Some of what opens today in church halls in the Eastern Cape will carry the weight the year deserves. Some will not. The court's silence will not determine which. The audience in the room will.
