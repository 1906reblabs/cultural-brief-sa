The Cultural Brief (SA)

"Freedom is indivisible, and when one person is enslaved, all are not free." — Nelson Mandela

This Saturday, South Africa marks Human Rights Day. The country will commemorate the sixty-sixth anniversary of the Sharpeville Massacre — 21 March 1960, when sixty-nine people were shot dead by apartheid police during a peaceful protest against pass laws. In Cape Town, the city will do what it does every Human Rights Day: hold a carnival. Floats, costumes, Zolani Mahola, DJ Ready D, thousands of people on the Green Point Fan Walk. Colour and movement and the deliberate, public performance of joy.

The juxtaposition is never uncomplicated. Human Rights Day is a day of mourning held in the register of celebration — a public holiday that carries both the weight of the dead and the obligation of the living to demonstrate that the rights those people died for have become real, durable, ordinary. The Cape Town Carnival understands this, even if it does not always say so plainly. Its theme this year — Follow Your heART — is lighter in framing than the occasion warrants, but the Carnival's structural logic is the right one: a free, open public event, shaped by community groups rather than corporate sponsors, on a route that anyone can attend without a ticket. That is a form of rights realisation.

But Human Rights Day 2026 arrives in a particular cultural context. The Bill of Rights — section by section the constitutional architecture that protects artistic freedom, freedom of expression, access to culture — turned thirty this year. The Constitution was signed by Nelson Mandela on 10 December 1996 at Sharpeville, returning to the site of the massacre to enact, in law, the rights that had been violently denied there. Oliewenhuis Art Museum in Bloemfontein, which opens a major exhibition this week under the theme Bill of Rights at 30: Making Human Dignity Real, understands the significance of the milestone.

The question this edition's long essay takes up — and that this week makes unavoidable — is whether thirty years on, those rights are holding. The Goliath affair has given the question a specific, legally tested form: what does section 16 of the Constitution, which guarantees freedom of artistic creativity, actually protect, when a minister decides a national platform should carry a different story, and a court declines to engage with the question? The Bill of Rights is thirty years old. This is a reasonable moment to ask what it is worth.

This Week's Cultural Signal

The Constitution is thirty years old. Human Rights Day falls on a Saturday, which means no substituted public holiday for most workers — a bureaucratic fact that has its own small poetry, given the history. The rights exist. The occasion is observed. What the Oliewenhuis exhibition asks, and what this week demands, is not whether rights are proclaimed but whether they are made real. That verb — made real — is doing significant work right now.

Curated Dispatches

Cape Town Carnival — Human Rights Day, Green Point Fan Walk (21 March)

The sixteenth Cape Town Carnival takes place this Saturday on the Green Point Fan Walk, under the 2026 theme Follow Your heART. Villages open at 3pm; the opening ceremony begins at 6pm; the themed parade moves off at 6:45pm. Entry is free and open to all, with limited VIP and seated stand tickets available.

Among the twelve Carnival Champions leading the procession are singer Zolani Mahola, rising talent Anslin Gysman, DJ Ready D, and performer and presenter Natasha van der Merwe. Activist Nyaniso Tutu-Burris, performer Dylan Majavie, and creative voices Jemma Fourie, Terry Fortune and Qhayiya Tyali each contribute to a Carnival shaped as much by lived experience as by performance.

What is worth noting, in the context of this week and this edition, is how the Carnival is funded. The 2026 theme arrives at a crucial moment for the creative sector, given the withdrawal of national government funding and other large-scale creative ventures across the country. That framing — a city-level cultural infrastructure event filling a space left by national withdrawal — is not incidental. It is the pattern of this moment: municipalities and community organisations sustaining what central government has stepped back from.

For months, community groups, dancers, artisans and designers have been working across the city — building, rehearsing, refining. That labour is the Carnival. The spectacle is its outcome, not its substance.

Green Point Fan Walk, Cape Town. Saturday 21 March, villages from 3pm, parade 6:45pm. Free. capetowncarnival.com.

Àṣẹ — Oliewenhuis Art Museum, Bloemfontein (Opens Thursday)

Oliewenhuis Art Museum opens Àṣẹ this Thursday — an exhibition running from 19 March through to 16 June 2026. The title draws from Yoruba philosophical and spiritual tradition: àṣẹ refers to the vital life force, the power to make things happen, to bring intention into form, and to transform thought into reality.

Curated by the current Art Museum Guides of Oliewenhuis, and drawing from collections at the museum and Art Bank of South Africa, the exhibition works across five interconnected themes examining how contemporary artistic practices remain grounded in human experience, and how art adapts to evolving tools, technologies, and social realities — revealing it as a critical site for reflection, resistance, and transformation.

The choice to anchor the programme in the Yoruba concept of àṣẹ — rather than in the more familiar registers of South African protest or post-apartheid reconciliation — is an editorial statement about which lineages of meaning are worth drawing on when thinking about art, rights, and the capacity of human beings to transform reality. It is also a refusal of the assumption, still widespread, that the relevant cosmological frameworks for South African contemporary art are exclusively either Western or narrowly South African.

Oliewenhuis Art Museum, Bloemfontein. Opens Thursday 19 March, runs to 16 June 2026. Free entry on Human Rights Day, 21 March.

Making It! 2026 — Craft and Design Institute, Kramerville (24–25 March)

On 24–25 March, the conference Making It! returns to the Kramerville Design District, marking 25 years of work by the Craft and Design Institute in supporting South Africa's craft and design sector. When the institute began in 2001, it worked with just 63 makers. Today, that network includes more than 8,300 creative enterprises across all nine provinces.

The conference is worth attending for its specific quality of ambition. South Africa's creative economy conversation has historically oscillated between developmental framing (crafts as poverty relief) and aspirational framing (design as luxury export). The CDI's twenty-five years have been an attempt to hold a third position: creative enterprise as a disciplined, sustainable economic sector, with its own logic, its own requirements, and its own claim on institutional support. Speakers include Wacy Zacarias, Mozambican artist and textile designer whose practice bridges regenerative textiles and storytelling through cloth; and Mbali Mthethwa, founder and creative director of The Herd, who reimagines traditional beading as a living language of identity and place. Shado Twala serves as MC.

Kramerville Design District, Johannesburg. 24–25 March. Virtual from R850, in-person from R2,500. CDI members receive a 20% discount. Tickets via Quicket.

KKNK Preview — Klein Karoo Nasionale Kunstefees, Oudtshoorn (Begins 28 March)

The Klein Karoo Nasionale Kunstefees opens in Oudtshoorn next Saturday, 28 March, and runs through to 4 April. The Afrikaans arts festival brings over a hundred theatre, music, and visual arts productions alongside markets and entertainment to the Klein Karoo — the small town briefly and dramatically doubling in population, as it does annually.

The KKNK occupies a specific, contested position in the South African cultural calendar. It is the largest Afrikaans-medium arts festival in the country, which means it carries the weight of Afrikaans culture's complicated negotiation with its own history — a language that was the instrument of apartheid's administrative apparatus, and also the medium of resistance for Breyten Breytenbach, Adam Small, Antjie Krog, Ingrid Jonker. The festival has, over three decades, made gradual and not always comfortable progress toward programming that reflects the full demographic of Afrikaans speakers — who are predominantly Coloured South Africans, not white — and toward productions that engage with the language's contradictions rather than papering over them.

Whether this year's programme achieves that is worth assessing in person. For serious theatre-goers prepared to make the journey to the Karoo, the KKNK remains one of the more concentrated weeks of new South African theatrical work available anywhere in the calendar year.

KKNK, Oudtshoorn, 28 March – 4 April. Programme and tickets at kknk.co.za.

Long Essay

The Bill of Rights at Thirty: What the Constitution Promised South African Cultural Life, and What We Have

In December 1996, Nelson Mandela signed South Africa's Constitution into law at Sharpeville. The choice of location was not accidental. Sharpeville was where, thirty-six years earlier, the apartheid state had demonstrated its most naked logic: people gathered peacefully to claim a right, and the state responded by killing sixty-nine of them. To sign the Constitution there was to inscribe its contents — its rights, its protections, its vision of what South Africa was choosing to become — at the site of the massacre. It was a statement about what those rights had cost, and who had paid.

The Bill of Rights, which forms Chapter 2 of the Constitution, is one of the most comprehensive rights instruments in the world. Section 16 guarantees freedom of expression, which the Constitution defines to include freedom of artistic creativity. Section 30 protects every person's right to use the language and participate in the cultural life of their choice. Section 31 protects communities from being denied the right to enjoy their culture, practice their religion, and use their language. These provisions were not accidental inclusions. They were hard-won by people who understood, from direct experience, what the suppression of cultural expression felt like and what it was for.

The Bill of Rights turns thirty this year. Human Rights Day arrives, as it always does, at Sharpeville and in Cape Town and in the communities whose cultural institutions mark the day with exhibitions, performances, open doors. And it arrives, in 2026, at a particular moment of testing.

The Goliath affair has been the cultural rights event of this year so far. Gabrielle Goliath and Ingrid Masondo — an artist and a curator selected by an independent committee to represent South Africa at the Venice Biennale — had their work cancelled by a minister who found its political content, specifically its mourning of Palestinian women killed in a genocide, inconvenient. They took the case to the North Gauteng High Court. The court dismissed their application without providing reasons. The Venice pavilion will be empty.

The rights dimension of this case was argued clearly and publicly. Goliath and Masondo's lawyers presented section 16 arguments: that the minister's intervention violated the artist's constitutional right to freedom of artistic creativity. The court did not engage with those arguments. It decided the case on contractual grounds — that the minister had sufficient contractual authority to terminate the arrangement — and left the constitutional questions untouched.

Leaving constitutional questions untouched is, in constitutional law, a decision. When a court declines to engage with rights arguments, it does not preserve the rights; it leaves the infringement standing. The minister's content veto was upheld by a court that refused to call it by its constitutional name.

It is worth being precise about what the Constitution promises. Section 16(1)(c) reads: "Everyone has the right to freedom of expression, which includes freedom of artistic creativity." The right is unambiguous. The constitutional text offers no ministerial exception; it does not say that artistic creativity is free unless the minister finds it divisive. The protection is general.

But rights, as South Africa's constitutional scholars have consistently argued, are not self-executing. They require courts willing to apply them, institutions designed to honour them, and a political culture that understands them as constraints on power rather than aspirations toward it. The thirty-year question is not whether the rights are inscribed — they are, in language that is among the most progressive in the world. The thirty-year question is whether the infrastructure for realising those rights has been built.

In some respects, the answer is clearly yes. South Africa has a Constitutional Court that has, over three decades, produced a body of jurisprudence on rights that is internationally significant. The court has found for plaintiffs in landmark cases on housing, health care, sexual orientation, and the rights of prisoners. The Bill of Rights has been invoked successfully in contexts that would have been unimaginable before 1996.

In the cultural domain specifically, the record is more mixed. The legal framework for cultural rights — the Copyright Act, the Performers' Protection Act, arts funding legislation, the governance structures of national arts bodies — has been criticised consistently for failing to keep pace with the practical needs of a creative sector operating in a changed economy. The National Arts Council, the body responsible for distributing public arts funding, has been through cycles of governance crisis that have disrupted funding pipelines and left practitioners without support at critical moments. The department responsible for arts and culture has, under successive ministers, demonstrated a poor understanding of what editorial independence means and why it matters for a national cultural representation.

Against this background, the Goliath affair is not an aberration. It is a clarifying event — one that makes legible a set of structural conditions that have been present for some time.

The first is the gap between the rights the Constitution proclaims and the legal mechanisms available to enforce them when the state itself is the infringer. Goliath and Masondo had constitutional rights. They could not, within the timeframe available, get a court to protect those rights. The legal process moved at the speed of courts; the cultural damage moved at the speed of a ministerial letter.

The second is the question of what happens to cultural rights when institutional design fails to protect them. The Venice Biennale pavilion selection process was designed to insulate curatorial decisions from political interference. That design proved insufficient. The minister's contract apparently contained no provision explicitly limiting his authority over the pavilion's artistic content. The gap was structural, not accidental.

The third is the question of where the sector itself stands when individual rights are threatened. The response from South African arts organisations and practitioners to the Goliath cancellation was substantial — statements, petitions, public expressions of solidarity. It was not enough. Moral authority, in the absence of legal authority and institutional protection, cannot compel a minister to reverse a decision.

What would it mean to make the Bill of Rights real — specifically its cultural provisions — at thirty years? It would mean, at minimum, governance structures for national cultural representations that contain explicit, legally enforceable provisions limiting ministerial content interference. It would mean arts funding legislation robust enough to protect the independence of funding bodies from political manipulation. It would mean courts willing to engage with section 16 arguments on their merits, rather than resolving rights cases on narrower contractual grounds when the constitutional dimensions are squarely presented. The Goliath case was dismissed without reasons. That opacity — in a constitutional democracy, on a rights question — is itself a governance failure.

Human Rights Day was created for a reason. The reason is Sharpeville — sixty-nine people shot dead for claiming rights. The day exists to remind the country of the cost of those rights, and to ask whether they are being honoured. This year, the Bill of Rights itself turns thirty. Oliewenhuis Art Museum opens an exhibition about making human dignity real. The Cape Town Carnival processes through Green Point under the theme Follow Your heART, drawing on the logic that public, open, community-made cultural events are themselves a form of rights realisation.

All of this is true, and worth attending to. And the Venice pavilion is empty, the court gave no reasons, and the minister has not resolved the contradiction. The rights are thirty years old. The question of what they are worth in the cultural domain — not in aspiration, but in the specific case where a minister decides an artist's mourning is too inconvenient for a national platform — has been posed this year in terms that are unusually clear. It has not yet been answered.

Sixty-nine people were shot at Sharpeville on a Saturday morning. Many of them were shot in the back, which means they were running away. The pass laws they were protesting have been gone for more than thirty years. The right not to be shot while peacefully exercising a constitutionally protected freedom is, in theory, absolute.

What gets tested, in 2026, is not the right not to be shot. It is the right to say, in art, in the name of your country, things that the state would prefer not to be said.

The distance between those two tests measures something real.
