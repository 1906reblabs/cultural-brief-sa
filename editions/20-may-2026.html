What Institutions Do With Silence: The Zeitz Board, the NAC, and the Governance Question That Will Not Age

Africa Day is five days away. The Venice Biennale has been open eleven days, and the Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa still has no named permanent successor. The National Arts Festival programme is confirmed for Makhanda. In Johannesburg, the Market Theatre marks fifty years. This week the question is no longer about individual failures. It is about whether the institutions that South African cultural life depends on are capable of governing themselves in public.

“The most common form of despair is not believing that things can change.”
— Gramsci, Prison Notebooks, 1929–1935

Thursday in South Africa, and Africa Day is five days away. In Venice, the 61st Biennale has been open eleven days. The critical record is deepening: the reviews that have appeared since the preview week confirm what Koyo Kouoh composed, and they name, in the same breath, what South Africa withheld. In Cape Town, the Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa continues its seventeenth month without a public account of its own future. In Johannesburg, the Market Theatre marks its fiftieth year with a season that is among the most programmatically serious in its recent history. In Makhanda, in five weeks, the National Arts Festival will open the most concentrated fortnight of new South African performance work available anywhere in the calendar year.

These are not unrelated facts. They are the same fact expressed in different institutional registers: the question of what South African cultural institutions do when they are required to make consequential decisions in public. The Zeitz board has been sitting with a succession question for seventeen months. The National Arts Council has been in recurring governance crisis for years. The Department of Sport, Arts and Culture demonstrated, through the Venice pavilion collapse, what happens when an institution is not constitutionally constrained to protect curatorial independence. The Market Theatre, by contrast, has spent fifty years sustaining the kind of programming that the state repeatedly tries to suppress — and has done so through institutional confidence that is worth examining now, when the other institutions' confidence appears to be failing.

Africa Month is a reasonable occasion to take stock. The Gramsci line at the top of this edition is not defeatist — it is a diagnosis. The despair that does not believe things can change is the despair that expresses itself as institutional inertia: the board that defers indefinitely, the council that restructures without reforming, the department that cancels rather than confronts. That despair is not permanent. It is a choice that institutions make, repeatedly, until someone decides to make a different choice. The question this edition holds is whether any of the institutions currently in question have reached that decision point — and what the evidence, rather than the aspiration, shows.

---

## CULTURAL SIGNAL

The Zeitz MOCAA board has had seventeen months to answer a governance question the Venice Biennale has now made impossible to defer. The Market Theatre has spent fifty years answering the same governance question every week. The difference between the two institutions is not their resources or their international profile. It is the consistency with which one of them has decided, year after year, that the work comes first. That decision is available to every institution. It is not always taken.

**title:** The Market Theatre at Fifty — What Fifty Years of Institutional Confidence Looks Like **meta:** Newtown, Johannesburg · Ongoing season **body:** | The Market Theatre opened in 1976 in the Indian Market building in Newtown — a deliberate location, a deliberate choice of building, a deliberate act of institutional confidence in a Johannesburg that the apartheid state was actively working to racially segregate. It has been through governance crises that were different in kind from the current Zeitz crisis but comparable in pressure. The season is not celebratory in the sense of being self-congratulatory. It is celebratory in the sense of doing what the institution does, at the level it does it, in the year it happens to be fifty.

That is what institutional confidence looks like. It does not announce itself. It simply proceeds. **footer:** Market Theatre, Newtown, Johannesburg. Season programme and tickets at markettheatre.co.za.

## LONG ESSAY

**title:** What Institutions Do With Silence: On Governance, Accountability, and Why the Zeitz Question Is Everyone's Question **type:** Institutional argument

The South African National Arts Council was established by an Act of Parliament in 1997. Its mandate, as stated in the Act, is to support the arts in South Africa "through the development and promotion of the arts and through the disbursement of funds." In the twenty-nine years since its founding, the council has been through four major governance crises — each characterised by the same structural features: a breakdown of financial controls, a period of contested leadership, a restructuring that addressed symptoms without causes, and a resumption of operations that did not fundamentally change the conditions that produced the crisis. The most recent crisis, which disrupted the NAC's funding pipeline in 2023 and 2024, left practitioners without support at critical moments in their work cycles. It did not produce, as of this writing, a structural reform that would make the same conditions less likely to recur.

This is not an isolated failure. It is a pattern. And the pattern, which is visible across multiple South African cultural institutions, has a specific shape: institutions that are designed to serve the cultural sector, and that are funded in whole or part by public money, are not consistently held to public account for how they govern themselves. When they fail — and they fail regularly — the sector responds with alarm, the institution responds with restructuring, and the restructuring produces an institution that is superficially different from the one that failed. The underlying governance culture, which is what makes institutions capable of self-correction rather than just crisis management, is not addressed because it is harder to address than an organogram change.

The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa is a private institution. It does not receive the NAC's funding, is not governed by parliamentary oversight, and operates under the authority of its own board. In governance terms, it is structurally more independent than the public cultural institutions that surround it. This independence was one of the conditions that allowed Koyo Kouoh to do the work she did: she was not subject to the ministerial oversight that cancelled Gabrielle Goliath's Venice selection, and the institution she ran was not required to manage its curatorial programme in relation to any government's preferred cultural narrative. Her work at Zeitz was possible partly because of the institution's private governance structure.

That same governance structure is now producing the succession silence. There is no parliamentary question time for the Zeitz board. There is no public audit requirement for its appointment process. There is no statutory obligation to disclose a timeline or a process or a set of criteria. The board can deliberate in private for as long as it chooses, and it has done so for seventeen months. The silence that has been possible is a function of the same structural independence that made Kouoh's work possible. This is not a paradox. It is the specific governance condition of a private cultural institution: the freedom from political interference that protects its programme is the same freedom from public accountability that allows its board to defer indefinitely.

The question is what fills the space between those two freedoms. In Kouoh's case, what filled it was intellectual seriousness, institutional confidence, and a clear account of what the museum was for. Those qualities were not produced by the governance structure; they were produced by her. The governance structure created the conditions in which those qualities could operate without interference. Now that she is gone, the question is whether the board understands the difference between the conditions it created and the content those conditions made possible. An appointment made for safety — for international credential and market fluency — would produce the conditions without the content. The form of the institution would continue; the argument it made would not.

The Market Theatre's fifty years offer a different model. The theatre has operated throughout its history in conditions of greater public accountability and greater financial precarity than the Zeitz. It has received state funding — through the Department of Sport, Arts and Culture and its predecessors — and has therefore been subject to the political pressures that come with state funding. It has been through governance crises of its own. What it has demonstrated, across half a century, is that institutional confidence is a choice that has to be made repeatedly, at the level of programming decisions, rather than once at the level of governance structure. The Market Theatre has not been protected from political pressure by its governance model. It has resisted political pressure through the consistency of its commitment to the work.

The distinction matters because it locates the failure of institutional governance not in the structure of institutions but in the culture of the people who run them. The NAC's governance crises were not produced by the Act that established it; they were produced by boards and executives who did not hold the institution to the standard the Act required. The DSAC's Venice pavilion collapse was not produced by the department's mandate; it was produced by a minister who used his contractual authority in ways that the mandate's framers did not intend and that the courts chose not to constrain. The Zeitz succession silence is not produced by the board's legal authority to deliberate privately; it is produced by a board that has not yet understood — or has understood and chosen not to act on — the public obligation that comes with running the most significant contemporary art institution on the African continent.

Africa Day arrives on Monday. The aspiration of the OAU's founding — that African nations could collectively construct a different relationship with the world than the one colonial history had assigned them — is sixty-three years old. The cultural expression of that aspiration is visible in Venice right now, in the form of an exhibition conceived by an African woman from a Cape Town institutional home. The governance expression of that aspiration is more mixed: a department that cancelled the selection, a court that declined to engage with the constitutional arguments, a succession that seventeen months of board deliberation has not resolved.

The Gramsci diagnosis is precise: the despair that does not believe things can change expresses itself as institutional inertia. It is not malicious. It is the accumulated weight of decisions not made, reforms not completed, appointments deferred until the deferral itself becomes a kind of answer. The antidote is not optimism. It is the specific, located decision that things will be different — not in general, but in the appointment that is made in the next board meeting, in the governance reform that addresses causes rather than symptoms, in the programming choice that insists on the work even when the platform is uncomfortable with it.

These decisions are available to every institution mentioned in this edition. They are not always taken. The record of which institutions take them and which do not is the only reliable measure of what the South African cultural sector is actually building, as distinct from what it claims to be building. Africa Month is a reasonable time to look at the record honestly. The honest reading is neither hopeless nor reassured. It is the reading that keeps asking the question.

## CLOSING REFLECTION

The Market Theatre staged Barney Simon's *Woza Albert!* in 1981. The apartheid government found it politically threatening. The theatre staged it anyway, in Newtown, on a stage that the government had not expected to last. The theatre is fifty years old this year. The government is gone.

Governance decisions — the choice to programme the uncomfortable work, to make the appointment that serves the institution rather than the moment's convenience, to hold the board to the standard the institution claims — are made or deferred in the ordinary business of cultural institutions, week by week, year by year. The accumulation of those decisions is what institutions are. The Zeitz board will make its appointment. What it will have decided, in the process of making it, is what kind of institution it is willing to run. That decision is not reversible in the short term. It is the one the sector is watching for, and has been watching for, for seventeen months.

The Market Theatre at Fifty — What Fifty Years of Institutional Confidence Looks Like

Newtown, Johannesburg · Ongoing season

The Market Theatre opened in 1976 in the Indian Market building in Newtown — a deliberate location, a deliberate choice of building, a deliberate act of institutional confidence in a Johannesburg that the apartheid state was actively working to racially segregate. In its first decade the theatre staged work that the apartheid government found dangerous and that the rest of the world found essential: Athol Fugard, Barney Simon, Gibson Kente, the work that made South African theatre internationally legible as a form of resistance.

It has been through governance crises that were different in kind from the current Zeitz crisis but comparable in pressure. What it has not done, in fifty years, is stop programming work that makes the room uncomfortable. That consistency — not the celebrity of the work, not the international prizes, not the building's heritage status — is the institution's actual achievement.

The 2026 season marks the anniversary with a programme that includes the world premiere of a new commission by Lara Foot, a revival of Barney Simon's *Born in the RSA*, and the continuation of the research-based work that has defined the theatre's relationship to South African social history for the past two decades. The season is not celebratory in the sense of being self-congratulatory. It is celebratory in the sense of doing what the institution does, at the level it does it, in the year it happens to be fifty.

That is what institutional confidence looks like. It does not announce itself. It simply proceeds.

Africa Day — 25 May, and What the Month Has Shown

National · 25 May 2026 · Africa Month

Africa Day arrives on Monday — the sixty-third anniversary of the founding of the Organisation of African Unity in Addis Ababa on 25 May 1963. The official observances will be as they are each year: departmental programmes, a presidential address, the television coverage that marks the occasion without necessarily inhabiting it.

What is different about Africa Month 2026 is that it has happened in the same month that the Venice Biennale opened "In Minor Keys" — the first central exhibition in the Biennale's history conceived by an African curatorial intelligence working from an institutional home on the continent. The OAU was founded on the argument that African nations, acting collectively, could construct a different relationship with the world than the one colonial history had assigned them. Koyo Kouoh's Venice appointment was the most concentrated contemporary expression of that argument in the cultural domain: not African art asserting its right to be seen, but African curatorial intelligence setting the terms by which the world's most visible art event organised itself.

Africa Month is a reasonable occasion to hold those two facts together: the sixty-three years of political and cultural infrastructure-building that made Kouoh's appointment legible, and the South African governance failure that ensured the country was not present to represent itself beside it. The aspiration and the failure are not separate. They are the same story, in two different registers.

Long Essay

What Institutions Do With Silence: On Governance, Accountability, and Why the Zeitz Question Is Everyone's Question

The South African National Arts Council was established by an Act of Parliament in 1997. Its mandate, as stated in the Act, is to support the arts in South Africa "through the development and promotion of the arts and through the disbursement of funds." In the twenty-nine years since its founding, the council has been through four major governance crises — each characterised by the same structural features: a breakdown of financial controls, a period of contested leadership, a restructuring that addressed symptoms without causes, and a resumption of operations that did not fundamentally change the conditions that produced the crisis. The most recent crisis, which disrupted the NAC's funding pipeline in 2023 and 2024, left practitioners without support at critical moments in their work cycles. It did not produce, as of this writing, a structural reform that would make the same conditions less likely to recur.

This is not an isolated failure. It is a pattern. And the pattern, which is visible across multiple South African cultural institutions, has a specific shape: institutions that are designed to serve the cultural sector, and that are funded in whole or part by public money, are not consistently held to public account for how they govern themselves. When they fail — and they fail regularly — the sector responds with alarm, the institution responds with restructuring, and the restructuring produces an institution that is superficially different from the one that failed. The underlying governance culture, which is what makes institutions capable of self-correction rather than just crisis management, is not addressed because it is harder to address than an organogram change.

The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa is a private institution. It does not receive the NAC's funding, is not governed by parliamentary oversight, and operates under the authority of its own board. In governance terms, it is structurally more independent than the public cultural institutions that surround it. This independence was one of the conditions that allowed Koyo Kouoh to do the work she did: she was not subject to the ministerial oversight that cancelled Gabrielle Goliath's Venice selection, and the institution she ran was not required to manage its curatorial programme in relation to any government's preferred cultural narrative. Her work at Zeitz was possible partly because of the institution's private governance structure.

That same governance structure is now producing the succession silence. There is no parliamentary question time for the Zeitz board. There is no public audit requirement for its appointment process. There is no statutory obligation to disclose a timeline or a process or a set of criteria. The board can deliberate in private for as long as it chooses, and it has done so for seventeen months. The silence that has been possible is a function of the same structural independence that made Kouoh's work possible. This is not a paradox. It is the specific governance condition of a private cultural institution: the freedom from political interference that protects its programme is the same freedom from public accountability that allows its board to defer indefinitely.

The question is what fills the space between those two freedoms. In Kouoh's case, what filled it was intellectual seriousness, institutional confidence, and a clear account of what the museum was for. Those qualities were not produced by the governance structure; they were produced by her. The governance structure created the conditions in which those qualities could operate without interference. Now that she is gone, the question is whether the board understands the difference between the conditions it created and the content those conditions made possible. An appointment made for safety — for international credential and market fluency — would produce the conditions without the content. The form of the institution would continue; the argument it made would not.

The Market Theatre's fifty years offer a different model. The theatre has operated throughout its history in conditions of greater public accountability and greater financial precarity than the Zeitz. It has received state funding — through the Department of Sport, Arts and Culture and its predecessors — and has therefore been subject to the political pressures that come with state funding. It has been through governance crises of its own. What it has demonstrated, across half a century, is that institutional confidence is a choice that has to be made repeatedly, at the level of programming decisions, rather than once at the level of governance structure. The Market Theatre has not been protected from political pressure by its governance model. It has resisted political pressure through the consistency of its commitment to the work.

The distinction matters because it locates the failure of institutional governance not in the structure of institutions but in the culture of the people who run them. The NAC's governance crises were not produced by the Act that established it; they were produced by boards and executives who did not hold the institution to the standard the Act required. The DSAC's Venice pavilion collapse was not produced by the department's mandate; it was produced by a minister who used his contractual authority in ways that the mandate's framers did not intend and that the courts chose not to constrain. The Zeitz succession silence is not produced by the board's legal authority to deliberate privately; it is produced by a board that has not yet understood — or has understood and chosen not to act on — the public obligation that comes with running the most significant contemporary art institution on the African continent.

Africa Day arrives on Monday. The aspiration of the OAU's founding — that African nations could collectively construct a different relationship with the world than the one colonial history had assigned them — is sixty-three years old. The cultural expression of that aspiration is visible in Venice right now, in the form of an exhibition conceived by an African woman from a Cape Town institutional home. The governance expression of that aspiration is more mixed: a department that cancelled the selection, a court that declined to engage with the constitutional arguments, a succession that seventeen months of board deliberation has not resolved.

The Gramsci diagnosis is precise: the despair that does not believe things can change expresses itself as institutional inertia. It is not malicious. It is the accumulated weight of decisions not made, reforms not completed, appointments deferred until the deferral itself becomes a kind of answer. The antidote is not optimism. It is the specific, located decision that things will be different — not in general, but in the appointment that is made in the next board meeting, in the governance reform that addresses causes rather than symptoms, in the programming choice that insists on the work even when the platform is uncomfortable with it.

These decisions are available to every institution mentioned in this edition. They are not always taken. The record of which institutions take them and which do not is the only reliable measure of what the South African cultural sector is actually building, as distinct from what it claims to be building. Africa Month is a reasonable time to look at the record honestly. The honest reading is neither hopeless nor reassured. It is the reading that keeps asking the question.

## CLOSING REFLECTION

The Market Theatre staged Barney Simon's *Woza Albert!* in 1981. The apartheid government found it politically threatening. The theatre staged it anyway, in Newtown, on a stage that the government had not expected to last. The theatre is fifty years old this year. The government is gone.

Governance decisions — the choice to programme the uncomfortable work, to make the appointment that serves the institution rather than the moment's convenience, to hold the board to the standard the institution claims — are made or deferred in the ordinary business of cultural institutions, week by week, year by year. The accumulation of those decisions is what institutions are. The Zeitz board will make its appointment. What it will have decided, in the process of making it, is what kind of institution it is willing to run. That decision is not reversible in the short term. It is the one the sector is watching for, and has been watching for, for seventeen months.
