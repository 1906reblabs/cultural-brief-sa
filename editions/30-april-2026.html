What the Record Shows: The Franschhoek Literary Festival, the Archive, and What South African Literature Does With the Country It Comes From

The Franschhoek Literary Festival opens tomorrow in a winelands town built on a dispossession that its programme has never adequately named. Venice opens in nine days, carrying Koyo Kouoh's posthumous argument to the world. In Makhanda in June, the National Arts Festival will hold what the stage has always held in South Africa: the full record of what the country cannot say through any other means. This week attends to what literature keeps and what it discards — and why the difference is not academic.

"We need a literature that restores to us the full human complexity of our experience — not one that reduces us to the spectacular moment of our suffering."
— Njabulo S. Ndebele, Rediscovery of the Ordinary, 1991

Thursday in South Africa. Yesterday was the substituted public holiday for Freedom Day — the country observed Monday what the calendar placed on Sunday, which is to say the commemoration arrived, as this publication noted last week, at one remove from the occasion it was marking. That displacement is ordinary now. The country manages it with the practised ease of an institution that has learned to honour its founding commitments from a slight administrative distance.

Tomorrow the Franschhoek Literary Festival opens. Four days of literary programming in the Western Cape winelands, in a town whose Dutch name refers to the French corner — the valley where the Huguenot settlers arrived in 1688 and where the land they farmed was expropriated from Khoi communities whose own names for the landscape were not recorded in the colonial archive. The FLF has been held in Franschhoek since 1998. It is the most commercially successful literary festival in South Africa, which means it is also the one whose relationship to the literary culture it claims to represent is most worth examining.

The occasion for this edition's long essay is a specific session in the 2026 FLF programme: a conversation between Nomusa Makhubu and Premesh Lalu on the archive and its politics. Both are practitioners whose work sits at the intersection of institutional memory, dispossession, and what it means to produce knowledge about communities that did not choose to be known in the terms that archives impose on them. Their presence at a literary festival — rather than only in academic venues — is the kind of cross-register encounter that the FLF, at its best, enables. Their conversation is also, in the specific context of Franschhoek, an occasion for a harder question: what does the literary record of South Africa hold, and what has it allowed to disappear?

Venice opens in nine days. Koyo Kouoh's posthumous exhibition — "In Minor Keys," realised by five curatorial advisers from her working archive — will open to the world on 9 May in the Giardini and the Arsenale. The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa, the institution she built, will meet the opening of her posthumous Venice exhibition without a named permanent successor at its head. South Africa will not be present in the pavilion space beside it. These are the conditions in which this Thursday arrives.

The Ndebele line above is not a coincidence. He wrote it in 1984, in the essay that gave his collected work its title, arguing that South African literature had been so preoccupied with the spectacular confrontations of apartheid — the protest, the martyrdom, the totalising drama of oppression and resistance — that it had missed the ordinary textures of life that literature, at its most serious, exists to record. The argument was controversial then and remains unresolved now. It is the argument the FLF's most serious sessions will be making, and failing to make, across the winelands this weekend.

The Franschhoek Literary Festival opens tomorrow in a colonial archive that has never fully named what it displaced. The archive that Nomusa Makhubu and Premesh Lalu work in asks what institutional memory does with communities it was not designed to hold. In nine days, Koyo Kouoh's posthumous argument opens in Venice. The week is about what the record shows — and what it has decided not to keep.

Curated Dispatches

Franschhoek Literary Festival — The Archive as Literary Occasion

Franschhoek, Western Cape  ·  1–4 May 2026

The Franschhoek Literary Festival opens tomorrow and runs through Sunday — four days of literary programming across the winelands town's wine estate venues, public spaces, and hotel terraces. The 2026 programme is large and varied in the way the FLF's programme always is: commercially anchored at its centre, with sessions of genuine intellectual weight distributed through the weekend for readers willing to work the programme rather than accept it wholesale.

The session this publication foregrounds is the conversation between Nomusa Makhubu and Premesh Lalu, provisionally titled "The Archive and Its Politics." Makhubu is a Cape Town-based curator, writer, and visual artist whose practice engages directly with what institutional archives hold about Black South African communities — with what is preserved, what is suppressed, and what the act of preservation itself does to the communities whose histories it claims to protect. Lalu is a historian at the University of the Western Cape whose scholarship on colonial memory, postcolonial knowledge, and the politics of the past is among the most rigorous currently being produced in South Africa. Their conversation belongs in this programme not because it is unusual but because it is necessary: the archive question — whose stories are held, in what form, on whose terms — is not a specialist question. It is the question that runs beneath every literary festival's existence, including this one.

Franschhoek is a specific choice of location. The town's wine estates sit on land farmed by enslaved people brought from Madagascar, Indonesia, and the East African coast by the Dutch East India Company from the late seventeenth century onward. The Cape Malay communities descended from those enslaved people are part of the Afrikaans-speaking inheritance that the KKNK has been slowly reckoning with; the FLF, held in the same linguistic and spatial region, has been slower to reckon with the same history. That the most structurally significant session in the 2026 programme directly addresses the archive and its politics — in this specific location — is worth naming as editorial serendipity of the productive kind.

Other sessions deserving attention: Sisonke Msimang, whose essays on race and belonging represent some of the sharpest cultural criticism currently being produced in South Africa, appears in a conversation on narrative and national identity. Sally Andrew's fifth Tannie Maria novel, Wild Things Never Die, is represented in the programme — her capacity to build international readership from a distinctly Karoo location remains worth understanding as a structural observation about South African popular fiction's relationship to place. The FLF programme includes much else that is celebratory rather than analytical; the task is to work the programme with the editorial judgment the publication requires.

Franschhoek Literary Festival, Franschhoek, Western Cape. 1–4 May 2026. Programme and tickets at flf.co.za. Sessions of intellectual weight fill first.

Venice — Nine Days

Arts Policy  ·  Venice Biennale Preview 6–8 May, Opens 9 May

This is the last edition of The Cultural Brief (SA) before Venice opens. The preview days run from 6 to 8 May; the public opening is 9 May; the central exhibition "In Minor Keys" — the posthumous realisation of Koyo Kouoh's curatorial vision by five advisers working from her archive — opens in the Giardini on the same day. The next edition of this publication will be published after Venice has opened. By Thursday 7 May, the world will have had its first look at what Koyo Kouoh was building when she died.

What Venice will show: the most influential contemporary art event in the world, organised for the first time by a curatorial intelligence formed on the African continent. Kouoh's argument — that African art's formal ambition and cosmological range must be understood on its own epistemological terms rather than made legible to a Western gaze, and that the infrastructure for that understanding must be built from inside the continent rather than ratified from outside it — will be the argument the world's most concentrated art audience encounters in the Giardini. This is not a minor cultural event. It is the largest institutional expression of a position that Kouoh spent her career constructing, and that she did not live to see made fully visible.

What Venice will also show: South Africa's absence. The Arsenale pavilion space that should hold a South African voice will hold nothing. Gabrielle Goliath confirmed an alternative Venice location for Elegy; the work will exist in Venice regardless of the pavilion. What will not exist is the South African state's institutional presence in the world's most visible art forum, in the same month that the curatorial intelligence it housed and then failed to honour opens that forum's central exhibition. This specific coincidence will be visible to everyone at Venice who knows the context. Most of them will.

In Cape Town, the Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa enters the most significant days of its institutional life since its founding — the nine days before the posthumous opening of the most ambitious project of the person who built it. The institution is doing this without a named permanent successor at its head. The framing of that fact is not incidental. It is the most important institutional question in the South African cultural sector right now.

Venice Biennale Arte 2026. Preview days 6–8 May. Public opening 9 May. "In Minor Keys" at the Giardini. The next edition of this publication will cover the opening.

Zeitz MOCAA — The Institution in the Days Before the Opening

Cape Town  ·  V&A Waterfront  ·  Ongoing

Fifteen months since Koyo Kouoh died. Nine days until the posthumous Venice opening. The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa — the institution she led as Executive Director and Chief Curator from 2019 until her death on 10 May 2025 — has been operating in a leadership interregnum whose terms have not been publicly resolved. No permanent successor has been announced. The curatorial programme has continued, supported by staff who worked with Kouoh and who understand her institutional project from the inside.

The nine days between this Thursday and the Venice opening constitute a specific test for the institution. They are the nine days in which the world's most significant contemporary art event will prepare to present Kouoh's posthumous argument — and the institution she built will face that moment without the leader who made the argument and without a named successor who could speak to what the institution intends to do with her inheritance. The silence is not merely administrative. It is, in the context of what is about to open in Venice, an institutional statement that the sector has not yet been asked to read clearly.

Kouoh's argument was that Zeitz MOCAA's significance lay not in its architectural spectacle or collection size but in its capacity to produce a sustained argument about African art on African terms — to refuse the trophy-institution model and insist, instead, on intellectual infrastructure. Whether the institution is currently doing that, in the nine days before Venice opens, is a question that cannot be answered from outside its walls. It can only be answered by attending to the curatorial framing of what is currently on its walls — by asking, in front of the work, whether the argument is still being made.

For readers in Cape Town in the next nine days: the institution is worth visiting not only for what is on display but for what the framing reveals about how Zeitz is navigating this transition. The framing is always the argument. It was always the argument. It remains the most reliable indicator of what the institution believes it is for.

Zeitz MOCAA, V&A Waterfront, Cape Town. Open Tuesday–Sunday. zeitzmocaa.museum.

National Arts Festival — Makhanda, June  ·  Advance Notice

Makhanda (Grahamstown), Eastern Cape  ·  Late June 2026

The National Arts Festival opens in Makhanda in late June — the oldest and most programmatically serious of South Africa's major arts gatherings, held in a university town in the Eastern Cape that briefly, annually, becomes the most concentrated site of new South African theatrical and performance work in the country. The 2026 programme has not yet been fully announced, but advance notice is warranted now for a specific reason: the fringe programme, which is where the most formally ambitious and politically urgent new work typically appears, requires booking well in advance, and readers in the sector who plan to attend need that lead time.

Makhanda is worth attending to in its own right in the context of this publication's sustained interest in the relationship between cultural production and the physical spaces in which it occurs. The town is also the site of the 1820 Settler Monument — an institution whose relationship to the Eastern Cape's full cultural record is as contested as the FLF's relationship to Franschhoek's. The National Arts Festival has been held in Makhanda since 1974. Its history includes the apartheid-era restrictions on which artists could perform on which stages; it includes the post-1994 expansion that attempted to redress those restrictions; it includes the ongoing question of whether the festival's fringe programme — now one of the largest in the world — generates economic and cultural benefit for the town's majority Black communities or primarily serves visiting audiences. These questions are not separate from the question of what the festival programmes. They are the same question.

What makes the National Arts Festival editorially necessary for advance notice is its scale and duration: two weeks of new South African theatre, dance, music, visual art, and spoken word, at a density and seriousness that no other event in the calendar replicates. The programme, when announced, will be the most reliable record of what new South African performance work is being made in 2026 and what it is trying to say. This publication will cover it when the programme is confirmed.

National Arts Festival, Makhanda, Eastern Cape. Late June 2026 — specific dates to be confirmed. artsfestival.co.za. Fringe booking opens before main programme; early registration advised.

Long Essay

The Huguenot Monument and What It Did Not Record: On the FLF, the Archive, and What Literary Culture Decides to Keep

The Huguenot Monument in Franschhoek was unveiled on 17 April 1948 — six days before the National Party's election victory that institutionalised apartheid as official state policy. The timing was not causal; it was structural. South Africa was, in April 1948, a country that built monuments to European settlers and had as yet erected nothing to mark what their arrival had displaced. The monument celebrates the French Huguenots who arrived in the Cape Colony in 1688, fleeing religious persecution in France, and who were granted land in the valley that became known as Franschhoek — "the French corner." It does not record the Khoi communities who had named and used that valley before the settlers arrived, or the enslaved people — brought from Madagascar, Indonesia, the East African coast, and the Indian subcontinent by the Dutch East India Company — who cleared the land, built the wine estates, and made the Franschhoek that the monument celebrates possible.

The archive is a decision. It is not a neutral record of what happened; it is a set of choices about what deserves to be preserved, in what form, and under whose authority. The choices made by the Cape Colony's archives, and the choices reinforced by the South African archive in the century and a half since, reflect the priorities of the administrations that maintained them: they are detailed about the property transactions of settlers, the administrative logistics of slavery, the movements of colonial officials. They are sparse about the interior lives of enslaved people, the governance structures of Khoi communities before dispossession, the knowledge systems that were operating in the landscapes the settlers described as empty. The emptiness was not a fact of the landscape. It was a fact of the archive.

Nomusa Makhubu's practice is organised around this recognition. Her work — which spans curatorial projects, critical writing, and visual art — operates at the intersection of photography, institutional memory, and what she calls the politics of visibility: the question of which communities have been seen, and how, and under whose gaze, and what that seeing has cost them. She is interested specifically in the photographic archive — in the images of Black South African communities that were made by colonial and apartheid-era photographers, held in institutional collections, and deployed, across more than a century, to produce a version of those communities that served the ideological purposes of the administrations that commissioned the images rather than the purposes of the people in them.

The intervention her practice makes is not simply to recover suppressed images or to argue for a more inclusive archive. It is more structurally disruptive than that. She argues that the terms on which communities are held in archives — the categories used to classify them, the institutions trusted with their preservation, the publics for whom the preservation is undertaken — reproduce the same hierarchies that produced the archive's silences in the first instance. To add more images of Black communities to a colonial archive is not to change the archive; it is to give the archive more material to work with. The change requires questioning the archive's logic, not supplementing its contents.

Premesh Lalu's scholarship makes a parallel argument from inside the discipline of history. His work on the colonial archive — and specifically on the ways in which historical knowledge about the Cape has been structured by assumptions that the archives themselves were designed to reinforce — has been a sustained argument that the academic discipline of history cannot simply recover what the archive suppressed; it must also account for the archive's active role in producing the past it claims to document. The archive does not record what happened. It constructs a version of what happened that serves the purposes of the people who built it. Understanding this does not make historical knowledge impossible; it makes it more honest about what it is doing.

These arguments are not new to academic circles. What is new — or at least unusual — is their appearance at the Franschhoek Literary Festival, in Franschhoek, at this particular cultural moment. The FLF is not typically the venue for this kind of structural interrogation. Its programme is shaped primarily by the commercial logic of literary festivals: books recently published, authors available for travel, sessions that will sell tickets to audiences whose primary relationship to literature is one of pleasure rather than epistemological challenge. The festival does serious work. It also does a great deal of work that is primarily celebratory, primarily oriented toward the existing readership of South African literature, primarily concerned with what literary culture is rather than what it is doing and to whom.

The Makhubu/Lalu session is different in kind from most of the FLF's programme, and its placement in Franschhoek is the kind of specificity that a literary festival can generate when its programming is attentive to location rather than merely using location as backdrop. The question about what the archive holds is not a general question in Franschhoek. It is a local question: this specific valley, these specific wine estates, this specific Huguenot Monument, this specific absence of any comparable monument to the enslaved people who built the valley that the monument celebrates. The archive in question is not abstract. It is the ground on which the festival is held.

South African literature has had a complicated relationship with the archive question since before it had a vocabulary for it. Njabulo Ndebele's 1984 argument about the "spectacular" in South African fiction was, in part, an archive argument: he was observing that the literature the apartheid era had produced was shaped by the same totalising logic as the apartheid archive itself — that it categorised human beings by their relationship to the dominant political drama, and in doing so reproduced the spectacular structure of that drama even in the act of opposing it. The ordinary life of communities — their textures, contradictions, interior complexity, self-sufficient meaning — had been sacrificed to the necessity of representing the extraordinary condition of oppression. The spectacle had consumed the record.

What Makhubu's practice proposes, three decades after Ndebele's essay, is a form of literary and archival attention that is structurally different from both the spectacular and its critique. She is not interested in replacing one totalising narrative with another — in substituting the archive of oppression with an archive of resistance. She is interested in the specific, material, irreducible texture of the communities the archive has processed — in the faces in the photographs, the names in the captions, the spatial arrangements that the colonial photographer composed and that the community did not choose, the traces of a life that exceeds the categories the archive imposed on it.

That kind of attention is what the Franschhoek Literary Festival, at its best, can host. The festival brings together readers, writers, and thinkers in a physical space — in a valley with a specific history, in front of an archive that is visible to anyone who walks through the town — and asks them to pay attention to what is there. Whether the 2026 programme, in its full extent, does that — whether it attends to the Franschhoek that exists rather than the Franschhoek that is convenient for a literary weekend — is the question the Makhubu/Lalu session makes it possible to ask. The other sessions will answer it, by their presence or absence, in the ways that cultural programmes always answer structural questions: not by declaring anything, but by showing what they chose to hold and what they let go.

In nine days, Venice will open. Koyo Kouoh's posthumous argument will be made public, in the world's most visible art forum, for the first time at the scale she intended for it. Her argument — like Makhubu's, like Ndebele's, like Lalu's — is about the terms on which African cultural production enters institutional space. It is about whether the entry is on the institution's terms or on its own. That question does not resolve at the Venice opening. It does not resolve at the Franschhoek Literary Festival. It is asked, every year, in every cultural institution that must decide what to hold and what to let go. The archive is never closed. It is always in the process of deciding.

Closing Reflection

Miriam Tlali was the first Black South African woman to have a novel published in her own country. Muriel at Metropolitan appeared in 1975, seven years after she wrote it. The manuscript had circulated for seven years because publishers and censors found reasons — editorial, political, administrative — not to produce it. When it was finally published, it was published in an edited form that removed sections the publisher considered too dangerous. The full text as Tlali wrote it did not appear until years later. The archive of her work is not the work she made. It is what the archive decided to keep.

She died in 2017. The FLF has been held in Franschhoek for twenty-eight of the thirty-one years since 1995. The number of FLF sessions devoted to her work and the structural conditions that produced it is not a number this publication has been able to verify. The archive may hold that information. Or it may have decided not to.
