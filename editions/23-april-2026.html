The Ground and What It Holds: AfrikaBurn, the Tankwa Karoo, and the Question of Who Belongs in a Landscape

In four days, on Freedom Day, tens of thousands of people will arrive in the Tankwa Karoo to build a temporary city on land whose communities' claim to the ground has never been adequately settled. The Venice Biennale preview opens in thirteen days — and South Africa will not be there. In Franschhoek, in nine days, writers will gather to argue about what archives hold and who gets to decide. This week is the last edition before the country marks thirty-two years of democracy. The ground beneath the celebration has not been surveyed honestly enough.

"We belong to the land. It does not belong to us."
— Es'kia Mphahlele, Afrika My Music, 1984

Thursday in South Africa. Four days from now, Freedom Day. The thirty-second anniversary of the first democratic election — the day nineteen million people stood in queues for hours to vote for the first time. It falls on a Sunday this year, which means it will be observed on Monday, which means the commemoration arrives slightly sideways from the day itself. The bureaucratic displacement has its own small meaning: the country is practised at holding its freedoms at one remove from their occasion.

In the Tankwa Karoo, on Sunday morning, AfrikaBurn will begin. The annual gathering in the Northern Cape desert — nineteen years old, drawing tens of thousands of participants to a semi-arid landscape approximately 350 kilometres north of Cape Town — opens on Freedom Day itself for the first time in its history. The coincidence of dates is not editorial, but it is real, and this edition treats it as such: not because AfrikaBurn is a Freedom Day event, but because the desert it is built in carries a Freedom Day question that the gathering does not always ask about itself.

The Tankwa Karoo is not empty. It has never been empty. The !Khomani San and Mier communities whose ancestral land includes the Tankwa were dispossessed through a series of processes that the democratic state has partially acknowledged through land restitution settlements and did not fully reverse. The Northern Cape desert was also where the South African government settled !Xun and Khwe soldiers and their families after the Border War, displacing them from their Angolan and Namibian homelands into an environment they did not choose. These communities are not incidental to the Tankwa's landscape; they are its human record. The land carries them whether or not the gatherings that use it name them.

This edition's long essay holds the question that the desert poses. AfrikaBurn is not reducible to that question — it is a complex cultural event with a serious installation programme that at its best produces work of genuine ambition. The 2026 poster artist is Fazel Ockhuys. Several installations this year engage the archival and community-based processes that connect specifically to the Karoo's layered histories. That work deserves to be attended to on its own terms. But it can only be attended to honestly if the ground it stands on is also attended to — if the freedom being performed on Freedom Day is understood as being performed on a specific piece of earth, not an abstract canvas.

Venice opens in thirteen days. The Franschhoek Literary Festival in nine. The week ahead is dense with cultural weight, and this edition moves through it with the care that density requires. The Mphahlele line above is not ornament: it is the epistemological position from which the edition proceeds.

AfrikaBurn opens on Freedom Day in a desert that carries the dispossession of the !Khomani San, the displacement of the !Xun and Khwe, and the ongoing question of what it means to build a temporary community of radical self-expression on land whose permanent communities were never adequately heard. The performance of freedom and the politics of the ground it is performed on are not separate questions. This week, they are the same question.

Curated Dispatches

AfrikaBurn — Tankwa Karoo, 27 April–3 May

Northern Cape  ·  Opens 27 April  ·  Tickets required

AfrikaBurn opens Sunday in the Tankwa Karoo — its nineteenth year, its first opening on Freedom Day. The gathering that runs from 27 April through 3 May is, at its best, South Africa's most serious annual experiment in non-transactional creative community: a week in which participants build, gift, and dismantle without money changing hands, in a landscape that makes the logic of the city temporarily unavailable.

The 2026 poster artist is Fazel Ockhuys, a Cape Town-based illustrator and visual artist whose practice engages memory, landscape, and the layered histories of the Western and Northern Cape. The large-scale installation programme — the heart of what AfrikaBurn is worth attending to — includes this year several works produced in dialogue with archival and community-based processes that connect directly to the Karoo's histories of displacement. This is the dimension of AfrikaBurn that the spectacular coverage misses: the sculptural and immersive works that, at their most serious, ask what it means to build something temporary in a place whose permanent human record has been treated as if it were also temporary.

This publication has approached AfrikaBurn with caution across three editions of advance notice, and that caution is maintained here. The event is large, it generates hype in inverse proportion to its actual substance, and the coverage it typically receives focuses on the costume and the sound infrastructure rather than the installation programme and the epistemological questions the desert itself poses. Readers who are there: the morning hours, before the programme's energy concentrates, are when the large-scale works can be encountered closest to their own terms. Readers who are not: the installation programme documentation, when published after the event, is the record worth seeking.

What is worth naming plainly, in this specific edition, on the eve of Freedom Day: AfrikaBurn is built on land that has a contested human history. The gathering that calls itself a temporary autonomous zone is temporary in ways the !Khomani San communities, for whom the Tankwa is not temporary, are not. Whether the gathering acknowledges that — whether its art programme engages it, whether its governance structures reflect any form of obligation to the landscape's permanent communities — is a question the event's promotional materials do not answer, and that the installation programme, at its best, is beginning to ask.

AfrikaBurn, Tankwa Karoo, Northern Cape. 27 April – 3 May 2026. Gates open Sunday 27 April. afrikaburn.org. Limited tickets remaining.

Franschhoek Literary Festival — What the Archives Hold

Franschhoek, Western Cape  ·  1–4 May 2026  ·  Nine days away

The Franschhoek Literary Festival opens in nine days — four days of literary programming in the Western Cape winelands with a programme that ranges, as it always does, from the genuinely serious to the commercially necessary. For readers of this publication, the task is to work the programme rather than accept it wholesale. Two sessions warrant specific attention this year.

The first is the conversation between Nomusa Makhubu and Premesh Lalu on the archive and its politics. Makhubu — whose practice as a curator, writer, and visual artist engages directly with what institutions hold about communities that did not choose those archives — and Lalu, a historian at the University of the Western Cape whose scholarship on memory, colonialism, and the archive is among the most rigorous being produced in South Africa, are a pairing that belongs in an academic symposium and has instead been placed at a literary festival. That displacement is the point: the questions they work in are not specialist questions. They are questions about whose histories are legible, whose are suppressed, and what institutions do with the difference. In the week before Freedom Day, in a festival held in a winelands town whose Dutch colonial architecture makes its own argument, that conversation is precisely located.

The second is the sessions featuring Sisonke Msimang, whose essays on race, belonging, and what South Africa asks of its citizens represent some of the sharpest cultural criticism being produced in the country. Her presence at the FLF is an opportunity for the kind of encounter between literary and political analysis that the festival, at its best, has always been able to host.

Sally Andrew's fifth Tannie Maria novel, Wild Things Never Die, was published this month and Andrew appears in the programme. As noted in the previous edition: the Tannie Maria series' capacity to build international readership from a distinctly South African location, without institutional support or prize validation, is worth understanding structurally. The FLF session will be celebratory; the structural observation is the useful one.

Franschhoek Literary Festival, Franschhoek, Western Cape. 1–4 May 2026. Programme and tickets at flf.co.za. Book early; the sessions of intellectual weight fill first.

Venice Biennale — Thirteen Days

Arts Policy  ·  Venice Biennale Preview 6–8 May, Opens 9 May

The Venice Biennale preview opens in thirteen days. The main exhibition — "In Minor Keys," the posthumous realisation of Koyo Kouoh's curatorial vision by five advisers working from her archive — opens to the world in sixteen. South Africa's pavilion space in the Arsenale will be empty. This is the last edition of The Cultural Brief (SA) before Venice opens, and what the opening will reveal deserves to be named clearly from this side of it.

What Venice will show: the most influential contemporary art event in the world, organised for the first time around a curatorial vision formed on the African continent, by a woman who built her institutional home in Cape Town, who died before she could see it realised. The central exhibition will carry Koyo Kouoh's intelligence into the Giardini and the Arsenale — her insistence on African art's formal ambition and cosmological range, her refusal to make that art legible to a Western gaze, her argument that the infrastructure for African art to speak on its own terms must be built from inside the continent rather than ratified from outside it. That argument will be made, in May, to the art world's most concentrated audience.

What Venice will also show: South Africa's absence. The Arsenale pavilion space is empty — the cumulative result of two failed processes, one ministerial and one institutional, both of which collapsed because the department responsible for the country's cultural representation at Venice could not sustain the independence of the curatorial process it was supposed to support. Gabrielle Goliath confirmed an alternative Venice space for Elegy; the work will exist. What will not exist is the South African state's presence, in the world's most visible art forum, in the same month that the curatorial intelligence it nurtured opens the central exhibition.

In Cape Town, the Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa — the institution Kouoh built, now in its fifteenth month without a named permanent successor — will be present in the work, absent in the space. That specific form of presence-in-absence is what this week, and the weeks of May and June that follow, will ask the sector to hold.

Venice Biennale Arte 2026. Preview days 6–8 May. Public opening 9 May. The central exhibition "In Minor Keys" opens at the Giardini. zeitz.mocaa.museum for Zeitz MOCAA programming through the Biennale period.

Nomusa Makhubu — The Archive as Argument

Cape Town  ·  Practice Profile  ·  Ahead of FLF, 1–4 May

Nomusa Makhubu is a Cape Town-based curator, writer, and visual artist whose practice sits at the intersection of photography, archive theory, and the politics of institutional memory. Her work — which includes essays, curatorial projects, and visual art that engages directly with what photographic and institutional archives hold about Black South African communities — has been building toward a form of critical practice that is recognisably distinct from both the protest tradition and the post-apartheid reconciliation aesthetic: it is, instead, a practice of sustained interrogation of the conditions under which images are made, collected, and deployed.

Her appearance at the Franschhoek Literary Festival alongside Premesh Lalu is worth attending to as a cultural event in its own right, not merely as part of the FLF programme. The question of what archives hold — and what they do not hold, and what that absence is doing — is continuous with the questions this publication has been tracking since the Goliath affair: whose mourning is permitted on a national platform, whose cultural production is preserved and whose is allowed to disappear, what institutions do with the materials they hold about communities that did not choose those archives.

Makhubu's forthcoming monograph, when published, will constitute one of the more significant interventions in South African visual culture discourse in recent years. Her practice models something the Zeitz MOCAA, in its current transitional period, will need to maintain if Koyo Kouoh's institutional argument is to survive the transition: the capacity to hold South African art to the standard of its own historical complexity, without reducing that complexity to a manageable narrative.

Nomusa Makhubu appears at the Franschhoek Literary Festival, 1–4 May 2026. Programme and session details at flf.co.za.

Long Essay

What the Tankwa Holds: On AfrikaBurn, the Desert, and the Limits of Temporary Freedom

The Tankwa Karoo is approximately 350 kilometres north of Cape Town, in the Northern Cape. It is a semi-arid desert — low scrub, pale dust, flat light, temperatures that drop below freezing at night and climb past 40 degrees Celsius in the afternoon heat. It receives roughly 100 millimetres of rain per year. The landscape resists habitation in most of its forms: it is not easily farmed, not easily built upon, not easily survived without sustained preparation. It is, in the language of government planning documents, marginal land.

That characterisation is historically loaded. The designation of land as "marginal" has been, throughout South African history, one of the primary mechanisms by which communities with deep and specific relationships to particular landscapes were dispossessed of them: if the land is unproductive, the argument ran, its current inhabitants are not really using it, and it can be reallocated for purposes that the state considers more productive. This logic was applied systematically to the Northern and Western Cape, where the !Khomani San — one of the oldest continuously inhabiting peoples on the African continent — were progressively pushed off their ancestral lands through the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. By the time the apartheid state formalised its spatial engineering in the 1950s, the process was already largely complete. The 1999 land restitution settlement, which returned a portion of the Kalahari Gemsbok National Park land to !Khomani San claimants, was a partial acknowledgement of a dispossession that was never fully reversed.

The Mier community — a Coloured community of mixed Khoi, San, Griqua, and other descent — were similarly dispossessed of land in the Northern Cape through processes that combined colonial expropriation, apartheid-era group areas legislation, and the designation of their land as appropriate for conservation purposes rather than human habitation. Their restitution claims, settled in the same period as the !Khomani San claims, restored some land and some rights. They did not restore what had been taken in the fullest sense.

The !Xun and Khwe communities arrived in the Tankwa region differently: they were brought there. During the South African Border War, !Xun and Khwe soldiers and trackers were recruited by the South African Defence Force from their homelands in Angola and Namibia, with promises that were never kept. After Namibian independence in 1990, they were relocated — many against their wishes — to Schmidtsdrift in the Northern Cape, and later to Platfontein near Kimberley. Their displacement from their ancestral environments was total. The SANDF compound at Schmidtsdrift sat on land that was itself contested; the relocation to Platfontein, when it finally came, took decades of advocacy and delivered conditions that fell well short of what was promised. These communities did not choose the Northern Cape desert. They were deposited in it by a state that had used them and then misplaced them.

AfrikaBurn has been held in the Tankwa Karoo since 2007. The event's founding philosophy — adapted from the Burning Man gathering held annually in Nevada's Black Rock Desert — emphasises radical self-reliance, gifting, decommodification, and the creation of a temporary community outside the social structures of everyday South African life. Its founders and participants have, since the beginning, described the Tankwa as a blank canvas: an empty space in which a different kind of community can be temporarily constructed. The language of emptiness is not malicious; it reflects the genuine experience of people arriving in the Karoo from Cape Town and Johannesburg, for whom the scale and stillness of the landscape feel like an absence of everything they know.

But the Tankwa is not empty. It has never been empty. The emptiness that AfrikaBurn's participants encounter is not a natural condition — it is the product of specific historical processes of dispossession and displacement, carried out over more than two centuries, that removed communities from the landscape and rendered their presence invisible. To arrive in the Tankwa and describe it as a blank canvas is to participate, probably unconsciously but not therefore innocently, in the ideology that made it appear blank in the first place.

This is not an argument against AfrikaBurn. The gathering produces genuine cultural work. The installation programme, at its best, engages precisely the questions of memory, displacement, and belonging that the landscape poses: several works in recent years have collaborated directly with the !Xun and Khwe artist communities, engaging archival materials and oral histories that the mainstream South African art world has systematically ignored. The 2026 poster artist, Fazel Ockhuys, works in a register that is attentive to the layered histories of the Cape and its hinterland. AfrikaBurn is not the enemy of the question the Tankwa asks. It is, at its most serious, a participant in it.

The problem is structural rather than individual. AfrikaBurn's governance model — the Binnekring, the Tankwa Town Council, the community-elected structures through which the event makes decisions — does not include the permanent human communities of the landscape in any systematic way. The event's lease arrangements with landowners in the Tankwa, and its relationships with conservation authorities, are not public. Whether the !Khomani San land restitution areas are proximate to the AfrikaBurn site, and whether those communities receive any benefit from the annual occupation of the Tankwa, is not information the event's public documentation addresses. The temporary community governs itself; the permanent communities remain outside its governance.

This is not, again, unique to AfrikaBurn. It is a structural condition of South African cultural life more broadly: the spaces in which creative community gathers are almost never the spaces in which the communities with the deepest claim to the underlying land have any formal role. The Company's Garden in Cape Town, the arts precincts in Johannesburg's inner city, the winelands venues in which the Franschhoek Literary Festival is held — all of these are built on land whose history of expropriation is present whether or not the gatherings that use the land name it.

What AfrikaBurn's Freedom Day opening makes available, in 2026, is a specific form of proximity: the gathering that claims to enact a different kind of freedom arrives on the same day that the country marks the political freedom that was supposed to address the dispossessions that made the landscape appear empty. The juxtaposition is not ironic in the easy sense. It is an opportunity for a harder question: what is the relationship between the freedom AfrikaBurn performs — the freedom to gather, to create, to refuse the commodity logic of ordinary South African life — and the freedom that Freedom Day proclaims? Are these expressions of the same project, or do they stand in tension with each other?

The tension is clearest on the question of land. Freedom Day commemorates a political transformation that included land reform as one of its stated obligations — the Constitution's section 25, the Restitution of Land Rights Act, the promise that the spatial engineering of apartheid would be systematically addressed. Thirty-two years on, that promise is incompletely kept. The majority of South Africa's agricultural land remains in the ownership of the minority that held it before 1994. The communities of the Northern Cape desert — the !Khomani San, the Mier, the !Xun and Khwe — have received partial restitution that did not restore what was taken. The land question is not resolved.

AfrikaBurn gathers on that unresolved land and performs, for seven days, a vision of community that does not require ownership, money, or the state's authorisation. The performance is genuine and, within its limits, valuable. But its limits are the limits of the temporary: on 4 May, the desert is dismantled and packed into trucks, and the Tankwa is left with the silence it carried before. The freedom AfrikaBurn performs does not survive the event. The land question it is staged on does.

The most serious installation works in the AfrikaBurn programme — the ones that engage the archival and community histories of the Karoo directly — are attempting something more durable than the event itself. They are attempting to leave something in the landscape: not a physical structure, but a record of having looked at the ground honestly, of having asked what it carries before building on it. That attempt is worth attending to carefully. Whether it constitutes a genuine encounter with the landscape's human history, or whether it remains a form of cultural tourism conducted on the margins of a large entertainment event, is the question that cannot be answered from this distance. It can only be answered in front of the work, in the morning, when the Tankwa is quiet and the installations can be encountered without the noise of the event obscuring what they are trying to say.

Freedom Day in South Africa marks the exercise of a right. AfrikaBurn, on the same day, marks the exercise of a different kind of freedom — one that is not inscribed in any constitution and not protected by any court. The desert holds both. What it has not yet been asked to hold, fully and honestly, is the question of whether the freedoms being exercised on it are available to the communities whose relationship to the land is the oldest and most specific, and whose claim to what the landscape holds has been the most consistently denied.

Closing Reflection

The !Khomani San have a word for the Tankwa: they have many words for it, in fact, in the N|uu language, a language with fewer than ten remaining speakers. The words name specific features of the landscape — not in the cartographic sense of labelling terrain, but in the sense of naming relationships: where the water comes from when it comes, where certain animals shelter, what the ground does after rain. That knowledge is not archived anywhere that AfrikaBurn has access to. It exists in a small number of living people, most of them elderly, in communities in the Northern Cape that are not adjacent to the AfrikaBurn site and are not part of its programme.

Freedom Day is four days away. Thirty-two years since the first democratic election. The ground the country stands on has not been surveyed, in the fullest sense, in any of those years. The survey would require listening to the people who know it most specifically. They have not been asked.
